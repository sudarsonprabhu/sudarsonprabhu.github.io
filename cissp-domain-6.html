Security Assessment and Testing

Proving that controls actually work — designing assessment, test, and audit strategies, then testing controls, collecting process data, analysing the results, and facilitating the audits that turn “we think we’re secure” into evidence.

Back to CISSP Notes

Sub-domains

Exam outline areas.

Domain 6 is broken into five exam outline areas that follow the assessment lifecycle — from designing a testing strategy, through running the tests and gathering the data, to analysing the output and running formal audits.

6.1 — Design & validate assessment, test & audit strategies

Building a strategy that decides what to test, how rigorously, and by whom — covering internal, external, and third-party assessments across on-premise, cloud, and hybrid environments, then validating that the strategy itself is sound.

6.2 — Conduct security control testing

The hands-on testing toolbox — vulnerability assessment, penetration testing, log review, synthetic transactions, code review & testing, misuse-case testing, test coverage analysis, interface testing, breach-attack simulations, and compliance checks.

6.3 — Collect security process data

Gathering operational evidence — account management, management review and approval, key performance and risk indicators (KPIs / KRIs), backup verification, training and awareness, and disaster-recovery / business-continuity data.

6.4 — Analyze test output & generate report

Turning raw findings into action — interpreting results, prioritising remediation, handling exceptions, and following ethical disclosure so issues reach the right people and actually get fixed.

6.5 — Conduct or facilitate security audits

Running the formal audits that provide assurance — internal, external, and third-party audits (including the SOC 1 / 2 / 3 report family and Type I vs Type II attestations) across every deployment location.

01

Why Domain 6 Matters — Assessment Strategy

Core questionAre our controls actually working?
Exam weight12% — roughly 24 questions
Key outputEvidence, not opinion
FoundationRisk management (Domain 1)

Domain 6 is the proof-of-work domain. Domains 1–5 tell you how to design and build security controls. Domain 6 asks the uncomfortable follow-up: how do you know those controls are working? The answer is a structured, repeatable assessment programme.

An assessment strategy is the master plan that decides what to test, how to test it, and how often. It must be grounded in the risk register from Domain 1 — there is no point testing low-risk, non-critical controls at the same intensity as controls that protect crown-jewel assets.

The assessment lifecycle has four phases that mirror the ISO 9000 Plan-Do-Check-Act loop: Plan (set goals, define scope, pick methods), Do (run the tests), Check (analyse results — this is most of Domain 6), and Act (management review and continuous improvement). Every topic in this domain slots into one of these four phases.

Exam tip: The CISSP exam loves to test the order of planning steps. The most important first step in planning any audit is setting clear goals. Without a well-defined scope and purpose, you cannot choose the right test type, interpret findings meaningfully, or write a useful report.
02

Security Audit Types — Internal, External & Third-Party

Internal auditFamiliar with systems; potential bias
External auditContract-driven; contractor checked by client
Third-party auditIndependent firm; broader experience, costlier
Test coverage% of system examined by a specific test

Before running a single scan, you must decide who will conduct the audit. The answer affects cost, credibility, depth of knowledge, and objectivity — all of which shape the value of the results.

Internal audits are performed by staff within the organisation. The biggest advantage is deep familiarity with the systems, architecture, and history. The biggest weakness is that familiarity can create blind spots — internal teams may not know the latest attack techniques used by adversaries against systems exactly like theirs. Internal audits also risk confirmation bias: people tend to look where they expect to find problems, and ignore unexpected attack surfaces.

External audits are triggered when a contract between two organisations includes security obligations. The contracting party — say, a bank that outsources payment processing — has the contractual right to audit the contractor to verify those obligations are being met. The audit scope is usually defined by the contract.

Third-party audits (independent audit firms) typically bring the broadest range of experience because they audit many different industries and environments. They have seen the attack vectors that internal teams may never encounter, and they bring objectivity that internal teams cannot. The trade-off is cost — these engagements can be expensive, and the auditors must spend time learning your environment from scratch.

Test coverage is the metric that describes what percentage of a system is actually examined by a given test. A vulnerability scanner that only covers the web DMZ has low test coverage. An assessment that includes internal network segments, end-points, databases, and APIs has high test coverage. Always ask: "what is out of scope?" because out-of-scope areas represent coverage gaps.

Exam tip: When the exam describes a scenario where an organisation wants an objective, unbiased assessment and cost is not the primary concern — the answer is almost always third-party audit. When the scenario says "our security team will evaluate our own systems" — that's internal audit.
03

Vulnerability Assessment — Identify, Define, Rank

PurposeFind & rank weaknesses — not exploit them
CVECommon Vulnerabilities & Exposures — names/IDs
CVSSSeverity score 0–10; drives prioritisation
SCAPAutomated checking protocol suite (NIST)

A vulnerability assessment is a systematic examination of a system to identify, define, and rank vulnerabilities. The key word is rank — not every vulnerability deserves the same urgent attention, and risk-based prioritisation is what separates a useful assessment from a raw scanner dump.

The industry uses three complementary standards to talk about vulnerabilities in a common language. CVE (Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures) is the naming standard — every known vulnerability gets a unique CVE identifier (e.g. CVE-2021-44228 for Log4Shell). This common naming prevents the confusion of different vendors calling the same flaw by different names.

CVSS (Common Vulnerability Scoring System) attaches a severity score from 0 to 10 to each CVE. A CVSS score of 9.8 (Critical) demands emergency patching; a score of 3.1 (Low) can be deferred to the next maintenance window. CVSS considers attack vector (network vs physical), complexity, privileges required, user interaction, confidentiality impact, integrity impact, and availability impact.

SCAP (Security Content Automation Protocol) is NIST's framework for automating the checking process. SCAP bundles standards like OVAL (Open Vulnerability and Assessment Language) and XCCDF (checklists) so that scanners can check systems against known vulnerability definitions in a machine-readable, automated way.

False positives vs false negatives are critical concepts in any automated scan. A false positive is when the scanner reports a vulnerability that does not actually exist — wastes remediation effort. A false negative is when the scanner misses a real vulnerability — creates dangerous false confidence. Both require manual analyst review to catch. Tune your scanner and validate findings before presenting them to leadership.

Exam tip — CVE vs CVSS vs SCAP: CVE = name/ID (what it is called). CVSS = score (how bad it is, 0–10). SCAP = automation protocol (how to check for it automatically). The exam tests these as distinct concepts.
04

Penetration Testing — Simulated Attack, Real Evidence

DefinitionSimulated attack at owner's request
VA vs Pen-testVA finds; pen-test actively exploits
5 phasesRecon → Enum → Analysis → Execution → Document
Rules of EngagementWritten scope, time window, emergency contact

A penetration test goes further than a vulnerability assessment. Where a VA identifies and ranks weaknesses, a pen test actively exploits them — just as a real attacker would — to determine the actual business impact. The critical legal requirement is that the testing must be explicitly authorised by the owner of the system. Pen testing without authorisation is a crime.

Phase 1 — Reconnaissance: The tester gathers intelligence about the target using passive (OSINT, Shodan, social media) or active (direct probing) techniques. This is where war dialing fits — dialling large blocks of phone numbers to find exposed modems or fax machines. Modern equivalents include scanning for exposed services on cloud ports.

Phase 2 — Enumeration: Active probing to map the attack surface — open ports, running services, software versions, OS fingerprinting. The goal is a precise inventory of what exists and can be targeted.

Phase 3 — Vulnerability Analysis: Matching the enumerated services against known weaknesses. This overlaps with vulnerability assessment but in the pen-test context the tester is specifically looking for exploitable attack paths, not just a ranked list.

Phase 4 — Execution: Actually exploiting vulnerabilities to demonstrate impact — gaining a shell, escalating privileges, moving laterally, or accessing sensitive data. Credential-based attacks like Pass-the-Hash (PtH) and Pass-the-Ticket (PtT) are commonly used here when credential scanning reveals NTLM hashes or Kerberos tickets.

Phase 5 — Document Findings: Producing a professional report (covered in Section 14) that translates technical exploits into business risk language, with recommendations ordered by severity.

Exam tip — VA vs Penetration Test: If the scenario says "identify and rank weaknesses" → Vulnerability Assessment. If it says "simulate an attacker" or "exploit vulnerabilities to prove impact" → Penetration Test. The explicit owner authorisation and active exploitation are the distinguishing factors.
05

Testing Perspectives — Black, White & Gray Box; Blind & Double-Blind

Black boxNo insider knowledge — pure outsider view
White boxFull knowledge of internals — deepest testing
Gray boxPartial knowledge — realistic insider threat
Double-blindStaff unaware + tester uses only public data

The perspective of a test describes how much knowledge the tester starts with. It dramatically changes what the test can find and what it costs in time.

Black box testing treats the target as a completely opaque system — exactly as an outside attacker would see it. The tester has no architecture diagrams, no source code, no credentials, no network maps. This produces the most realistic simulation of an external attacker but is the most time-consuming because the tester must discover everything from scratch.

White box testing gives the tester complete knowledge of internals before a single scan is run — architecture, source code, credentials, network diagrams, everything. This allows the deepest, most thorough testing in the shortest time but does not simulate an uninformed external attacker. White box is ideal for code reviews, logic flaw hunting, and audit efficiency.

Gray box testing sits in the middle — the tester has some but not all internal information, simulating an insider threat or a sophisticated attacker who has done significant reconnaissance. This is often the most practical choice balancing realism with efficiency.

Separately, tests can be categorised by whether the target's security team knows the test is happening. A blind test means the tester uses only publicly available information, and the target's security staff is notified. A double-blind test (stealth assessment) means the tester uses only public data and the security staff is not notified — this tests the team's detection and response capability in addition to the technical controls. Also called a "zero-knowledge" or "surprise" test.

Exam tip: "Blind" refers to the tester's knowledge of the target. "Double-blind" additionally means the target's staff don't know testing is occurring. These are not opposite ends of the same spectrum — they are two independent dimensions. Read exam questions carefully for which axis is being tested.
06

Specialised Testing Techniques

Synthetic transactionsScripted fake-user events; test service performance
Misuse casesUse-case with threat actor; models attacker goals
FuzzingRandom/malformed input injection; finds crashes
War dialingDial phone number ranges to find exposed modems

Beyond standard scans and pen tests, Domain 6 covers several specialised techniques that test specific aspects of a system's security.

Synthetic transactions are scripted events that mimic the behaviour of real users. A synthetic transaction might simulate logging in, placing an order, and logging out — all automatically, 24/7. The purpose is to test both performance (is the service responding within SLA?) and security (did the authentication step reject bad credentials?). Synthetic transactions run in controlled environments so the data generated is known and can be audited. They complement Real User Monitoring (RUM), which passively watches actual user interactions rather than scripted ones.

Misuse cases extend the software engineering concept of use cases by adding threat actors and the tasks they want to perform. A normal use case says "customer logs in and views balance." A misuse case says "attacker attempts SQL injection in the login field to extract the full customer table." Misuse cases drive security requirements during software development and focus penetration test planning.

Fuzzing (fuzz testing) involves feeding a system random, unexpected, or malformed input in large volumes to find crashes, memory corruption, buffer overflows, or unhandled exceptions. Fuzzers are largely automated. Static fuzzing uses a fixed set of known-bad inputs; dynamic fuzzing generates inputs based on observed system responses, adapting to find deeper bugs.

War dialing is the legacy technique of automatically dialling large blocks of phone numbers to find modems, fax machines, or remote-access servers. While seemingly outdated, it still appears on the CISSP exam and is conceptually important — modern equivalents include mass port scanning and internet-wide scanning (e.g. Shodan) for exposed industrial control system ports or default-credential devices.

Exam tip — Synthetic vs RUM: Synthetic transactions are scripted and controlled — you know exactly what inputs went in and you verify expected outputs. Real User Monitoring (RUM) passively observes actual user interactions. When an exam question asks about systematically testing whether a critical service is functioning correctly around the clock, the answer is synthetic transactions.
07

Code Review & Interface Testing

Code reviewSystematic exam of code by someone other than author
Static analysisAnalyse code without executing it (SAST)
Dynamic analysisTest running code (DAST) — finds runtime bugs
Interface testingEvaluate data exchange points — APIs, UIs, protocols

Code review is a systematic examination of software source code by someone other than the author. The "other than the author" requirement is critical — peer review removes the blind spots that come from writing the code yourself. It is the most cost-effective way to find vulnerabilities: it is far cheaper to find a SQL injection flaw in a code review during development than to discover it in production.

Code reviews come in several forms. A manual code review involves a skilled reviewer reading through code looking for logic flaws, missing input validation, insecure cryptographic choices, and access control bugs. Static analysis (SAST) automates this by scanning the source code without executing it — it can process millions of lines quickly but generates false positives that require human triage. Dynamic analysis (DAST) runs the compiled application and injects inputs to observe runtime behaviour — finding issues like race conditions that do not appear in static code.

Interface testing evaluates the security of exchange points — any place where data flows between systems or between a system and a user. This includes: APIs (do they enforce authentication and input validation?), user interfaces (are they vulnerable to XSS or CSRF?), and protocol interfaces (does the TLS handshake enforce strong cipher suites?). Interface testing asks: "what happens when unexpected or malicious data arrives at this exchange point?"

Boundary Value Analysis (BVA) is a specific interface-testing technique that focuses on the edges of acceptable input ranges. If a field accepts values 1–100, BVA tests 0, 1, 2, 99, 100, and 101 — the boundaries where logic errors most frequently occur.

Exam tip: "Conducted by someone other than the author" is the defining phrase for a formal code review. If an exam scenario describes a developer checking their own code, that is not a code review in the CISSP sense — that is self-review, which has no independent assurance value.
08

Log Review — What to Monitor and Why

DefinitionExamination of log files to detect security events
Monitor forErrors · Modifications · Breaches
Log size riskCircular overwrite can destroy evidence
Time syncNTP is mandatory for log correlation

A log review is the examination of system log files to detect security events or to verify the effectiveness of security controls. Logs are the audit trail of what actually happened on a system — they are the primary forensic evidence source when an incident occurs.

Security teams monitor logs for three major categories of events: Errors (authentication failures, application crashes, database errors that may indicate an injection attempt), Modifications (changes to sensitive files, privilege escalation, account creation — especially on weekends or after hours), and Breaches (successful unauthorised access, data exfiltration patterns, lateral movement indicators).

Log size management is a critical operational concern. Logs will grow indefinitely if not managed. Two common strategies: Circular overwrite — the log is treated as a ring buffer; when it fills, the oldest entries are overwritten. The risk is that old entries — which may be critical forensic evidence — are silently destroyed. Clipping levels — only log events that exceed a threshold (e.g., more than 5 failed logins) to reduce volume while keeping security-relevant signals. Both must be configured with care.

Timestamp consistency is non-negotiable for log correlation. Imagine trying to reconstruct an attack across a web server, a database, a firewall, and a domain controller — if each has a different system clock, correlating the sequence of events becomes nearly impossible. Network Time Protocol (NTP) synchronises all clocks to a common reference time, making multi-source log correlation reliable and legally defensible in court.

Exam tip — Circular overwrite risk: Circular overwrite is a data loss risk for forensic evidence, not a security control. If an exam scenario says "the organisation needs to ensure old log entries are not lost," the answer involves increasing log storage or implementing log shipping/archiving — not circular overwrite. NTP is the solution any time an exam mentions "log correlation" or "consistent timestamps."

SIEM — The Log-Management Lifecycle

SIEM purposeAggregate, normalise, correlate, alert on log data
7 stagesGenerate → Transmit → Collect → Normalise → Analyse → Retain → Dispose
NormalisationConvert all log formats to a common schema
RetentionLegal hold period; PCI-DSS = 1 yr; often 7 yrs

A SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) platform is the central nervous system of log management. It collects logs from every system across the enterprise — firewalls, endpoints, servers, cloud platforms, applications — and correlates them in real time to detect threats that no single log source would reveal alone.

Stage 1 — Generation: Log entries are created at the source — an OS kernel event, a firewall deny, a database query. Two key issues arise here: volume (high-activity systems generate enormous log volume) and what to record (clipping levels and circular overwrite, discussed in Section 08).

Stage 2 — Transmission: Log data is sent from sources to the central collection point. Use encrypted channels (TLS) to prevent tampering in transit. The protocol may be syslog, Windows Event Forwarding, or an agent-based collector.

Stage 3 — Collection / Aggregation: A log aggregator (often a syslog server or SIEM forwarder) receives logs from all sources, deduplicates, and buffers them before sending to the SIEM for processing.

Stage 4 — Normalisation: The most technically important stage. Every system logs in a different format — Windows Event Log, CEF, LEEF, plain syslog. Normalisation converts all formats to a common schema so that a firewall "deny" and an IDS "alert" can be correlated by the same rule. Without normalisation, correlation is impossible.

Stage 5 — Analysis: Correlation rules, anomaly detection, and threat intelligence feeds are applied to the normalised data stream. This is where alerts are generated. Modern SIEMs use machine learning to baseline normal behaviour and flag deviations.

Stage 6 — Retention: Logs must be kept for a defined period to meet legal, regulatory, and forensic requirements. PCI-DSS requires one year (three months immediately available). Many organisations retain security logs for three to seven years. Retention policies must be written and enforced.

Stage 7 — Disposal: When the retention period expires, logs must be securely destroyed — just like any other sensitive data. Logs often contain credentials, session tokens, IP addresses, and user behaviour that is personally identifiable. Simply deleting a file is not sufficient; use cryptographic erasure or certified media destruction.

Exam tip — Normalisation is the enabler: Without normalisation, a SIEM cannot correlate across vendors. The exam may present a scenario where a SIEM "cannot correlate events from different systems" — the answer is that normalisation is missing or misconfigured. Also remember: SIEM does both SIM (historical analysis) and SEM (real-time alerting) in one platform.

Continuous Monitoring — Always-On Security Visibility

Point-in-timeSnapshot — valid at moment of test only
ContinuousOngoing visibility — detects change in real time
RUMReal User Monitoring — passively observes actual users
Privileged accountsHigh-risk; must be continuously monitored

Traditional security assessments are point-in-time — like taking a photograph. The photo is accurate at the moment it was taken, but the environment changes immediately after. A firewall rule is added. A patch is not applied. An account is misconfigured. Continuous monitoring replaces the photograph with a live video feed.

NIST SP 800-137 defines a framework for Information Security Continuous Monitoring (ISCM). It involves: defining a monitoring strategy, establishing metrics, implementing monitoring (automated tools, SIEM, vulnerability scanners), analysing data, reporting findings, and responding. The cycle runs indefinitely — hence continuous.

Privileged user accounts represent the single highest risk in any environment. A compromised domain administrator can read every email, exfiltrate every database, and disable every control. Continuous monitoring of privileged account activity — after-hours logins, unusual command execution, accessing resources outside normal scope — is a critical detective control.

Real User Monitoring (RUM) passively captures the experience of actual users interacting with a system. Unlike synthetic transactions (scripted) RUM observes real traffic without altering it. RUM is important for detecting both performance degradation and anomalous user behaviour (a user downloading 10x their normal data volume may indicate insider threat or a compromised credential).

User account lifecycle monitoring is a subset of continuous monitoring that the CISSP exam explicitly tests. User accounts must be promptly suspended (not deleted — preserve evidence) whenever a user departs the organisation or takes an extended leave. Stale, orphaned accounts that remain active after an employee leaves are a common attack vector.

Exam tip — Suspend, not delete: When an employee leaves the organisation, the correct action is to suspend / disable the account immediately — not delete it. Deletion destroys the audit trail and may violate legal hold requirements. The account (and its data) may need to be preserved as evidence in a future investigation.

Security Metrics — KPIs, KRIs, and What They Measure

KPIHow effective are we? (Performance)
KRIHow risky is what we are doing? (Risk exposure)
Example KPI% patches applied within SLA; mean time to detect
Example KRI% systems running unsupported OS; # open critical CVEs

Metrics are how we demonstrate security value to leadership and drive continuous improvement. Two types dominate Domain 6: Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) and Key Risk Indicators (KRIs).

KPIs measure effectiveness — how well is the security function performing a given task at a given point in time? Examples: What percentage of critical patches were applied within the 30-day SLA? What is the mean time to detect (MTTD) an incident? What is the mean time to respond (MTTR)? What percentage of employees completed security awareness training? These are backward-looking — they measure what was achieved.

KRIs measure risk exposure — how dangerous is the current state of affairs? Examples: What percentage of production systems are running operating systems that are end-of-life (no patches available)? How many open critical CVSS 9+ vulnerabilities are in production? What is the number of privileged accounts without multi-factor authentication? KRIs are forward-looking — they signal where risk is building before it becomes an incident.

Both are essential inputs to the management review (Section 15). KPIs answer "how are we doing?" and KRIs answer "what could hurt us next?" Together they provide the data-driven picture senior leaders need to make strategic security investment decisions.

Exam tip — KPI vs KRI: The quickest test: if the metric describes past performance ("we patched 94% of critical CVEs within SLA") → KPI. If the metric describes current exposure to future harm ("23 end-of-life servers in production") → KRI. The exam will test this distinction directly.

SOC Reports — Third-Party Assurance

SOC 1Financial reporting controls (SSAE 18 / ISAE 3402)
SOC 2Security Trust Service Criteria — CIA + P + A
SOC 3Public-facing summary of SOC 2 — no restricted data
Type I vs IIDesign (point-in-time) vs Operating Effectiveness (period)

When you outsource a business function to a third-party service provider — a cloud host, a payroll processor, a SaaS vendor — you retain accountability for the data you handed them, even though you no longer have direct control. SOC (Service Organization Control) reports are the mechanism by which those service providers prove to you that their controls work.

SOC 1 reports assess controls that are relevant to financial reporting. They matter when the service provider processes transactions or data that affect your financial statements — a payroll processor or an accounts-payable outsourcer. SOC 1 is governed by SSAE 18 in the US (formerly SAS 70) and ISAE 3402 internationally.

SOC 2 reports assess controls relevant to the AICPA's Trust Service Criteria: Security (mandatory), Availability, Processing Integrity, Confidentiality, and Privacy (optional, included if applicable). SOC 2 is what you demand from a cloud provider or SaaS vendor when you want assurance about data security.

SOC 3 is a public-facing summary version of SOC 2. It contains the auditor's opinion (pass/fail) but not the detailed test results, findings, or control descriptions. Service providers can publish SOC 3 on their websites as a marketing trust signal. You, as a potential customer, cannot make informed procurement decisions on SOC 3 alone — you need SOC 2.

All three SOC report types come in Type I and Type II variants. A Type I report assesses whether controls are suitably designed at a single point in time — like a building inspection that confirms the fire sprinklers are installed correctly. A Type II report assesses whether controls operated effectively over a period of time (typically 6–12 months) — like reviewing video footage to confirm the sprinklers actually activated when triggered. Type II provides far stronger assurance.

Exam tip — Type I vs Type II memory trick: Type I = Instant snapshot — design only, single point in time. Type II = Time period — operating effectiveness over months. Regulators and sophisticated buyers always prefer Type II SOC 2. When a cloud vendor shows you only a SOC 3 or only a Type I report, ask for the Type II SOC 2.
13

Administrative Controls, BCP/DRP Testing & Social Engineering

Admin controlsPolicies & procedures — audited by review
Backup verificationBackups are useless without tested restores
BCP/DRP testTabletop → Walkthrough → Simulation → Full test
Phishing simulationTests awareness; click rate = KPI

Not all security controls are technical. Administrative controls — policies, procedures, training programmes, separation of duties, job rotation — are equally critical and must be assessed. Auditing administrative controls typically involves reviewing policy documents, interviewing staff, testing whether procedures are actually followed, and checking training completion records.

Privileged account testing is a high-priority administrative assessment. Auditors look at whether privileged accounts (domain admin, root, DBA) have MFA enforced, whether the principle of least privilege is applied (no one has more access than they need), whether session recordings exist, and whether privileged access is time-bound (just-in-time provisioning). Uncontrolled privileged accounts are among the most exploited attack vectors.

Data backup verification is a frequently overlooked but exam-critical concept: backups that have never been tested are not reliable. The CISSP exam makes this explicit — backups should not be considered reliable unless they have been verified to successfully restore data. Regular restore tests (to a non-production environment) are mandatory.

BCP/DRP testing assesses whether business continuity and disaster recovery plans actually work. Four test levels in increasing intensity: (1) Tabletop exercise — team discusses the scenario verbally; (2) Walkthrough / structured walk-through — team traces through the plan step by step; (3) Simulation — team acts as if the disaster occurred, stopping short of actual failover; (4) Full interruption test — actual failover to backup systems. Most disruptive; most realistic. BCP and DRP must be tested regularly because environments change.

Social engineering testing (phishing simulations, vishing calls, pretexting campaigns) tests the human layer. A phishing simulation sends fake phishing emails to employees and measures click rate and credential submission rate. These rates are KPIs for the security awareness programme. High click rates indicate training gaps. The test must be authorised by senior leadership and handled ethically — results are used for training, not punishment.

Exam tip — BCP vs DRP: BCP ensures critical business processes remain uninterrupted or are quickly restored. DRP ensures information systems supporting those processes remain operational or are quickly restored. BCP is broader; DRP is IT-focused. Both need periodic testing — a plan that has never been tested is not a plan, it is a document.

Writing Technical Reports & Executive Summaries

Report sectionsExec Summary · Background · Methodology · Findings · Recommendations · Appendices
Asset valuationCost · Income · Market approaches
Monetary riskAsset Value × Probability = Risk ($)
Audience ruleExecutives = business language; technicians = technical detail

A technical report is not a scanner printout. The CISSP exam explicitly tests the ability to communicate security findings to different audiences — a skill that distinguishes a senior security professional from a tool operator.

Six elements of a good technical audit report:

(1) Executive Summary — one to two pages maximum. Hard-hitting summary of key findings and recommendations in business language. Many senior executives will read only this section. Lead with the most impactful message.

(2) Background — why was this assessment conducted? What is the scope (and what is explicitly out of scope)? What policies, standards, or regulations does it reference?

(3) Methodology — how was the test conducted? (Audits must be repeatable.) Include personnel, dates, times, locations, tools used, and any exclusions.

(4) Findings — grouped logically (by business impact for executives, by technical class for engineers). Each finding must answer "so what?" — the business consequence, not just the technical description.

(5) Recommendations — mirrors the Findings structure. Answers "now what?" Make it actionable. When writing for a CFO who dislikes capital expenditure, frame expensive recommendations as operational cost savings rather than capital investments.

(6) Appendices — raw scanner output, evidence screenshots, network diagrams. Provides the technical depth and justification for the findings, without cluttering the main report.

Quantifying risk for the Executive Summary — expressing risk in monetary terms is the most persuasive way to get leadership attention. The basic formula: Risk ($) = Asset Value × Probability of Loss. For example: customer data valued at $1M with a 10% breach probability = $100,000 annual risk exposure. Three methods of asset valuation:

Cost approach — what did it cost to acquire or create the asset? Simple; good for tangible IT assets. Income approach — what expected revenue does the asset generate, divided by the capitalisation rate? Better for information assets with revenue contribution. Market approach — what would an equivalent asset fetch on the open market? Requires market transparency data.

Exam tip — auto-generated reports: The CISSP exam explicitly warns against reports that are nothing more than automated scanner output with no analysis. A good report tells a story grounded in empirical facts, tailored to its audience, and making a compelling argument for action. If the security posture is genuinely good, the report should say so — do not overemphasise minor findings to justify the engagement.

Management Review — The Act Phase of PDCA

PurposeSenior leadership decides on ISMS strategy
InputsAudit results · Open items · Customer feedback · Recommendations
OutputsApprove / Reject / Defer recommendations
FrequencyPeriodic; synced to implementation cycle length

A management review is a formal meeting of senior organisational leaders to determine whether the information security management system (ISMS) is effectively accomplishing its goals. It is the Act phase of the PDCA loop — the point where all the data gathered in the Check phase is converted into strategic decisions.

Who must attend: All key decision-makers. This is not a technical meeting — it is a strategic one. The ISMS gets its authority and resources from top-level buy-in. A management review that does not include the CISO, CIO, CFO, and relevant business unit leaders is undermined before it starts.

Inputs to the management review: (1) Audit results — both internal and external, with executive summaries; (2) Open items and action items from the previous management review — what was committed and whether it was delivered; (3) Customer feedback — satisfaction surveys, RUM data, social media sentiment; (4) Recommendations for improvement — proposed high-level changes needing leadership approval or resources.

Presenting options: The ISMS team typically presents three to five options for each major recommendation, ranging from "do nothing" (describing the risk of inaction) to "gold-plated response" (maximum investment). Each option is evaluated against objective criteria: monetary cost (life-cycle cost, not just acquisition), residual risk, operational impact, training requirements, and complexity. This allows leaders to make informed cost-benefit decisions.

Outputs: Leadership approves, rejects, approves with modifications, or defers recommendations, typically with a request for more data. All decisions become deliverables with owners and due dates. These deliverables become the open-items input to the next management review — closing the loop.

Frequency: The more immature the ISMS, the more frequently reviews should occur. The review cycle must be synchronised with implementation cycles — if a decision will take twelve months to implement and measure, reviewing every three months yields meaningless data. The review becomes proactive rather than reactive when conducted on a regular, pre-planned schedule.

Exam tip — life-cycle cost: When presenting recommendations to management, always use life-cycle cost (acquisition + implementation + training + maintenance over the system's lifetime), not just the purchase price. A $50,000 tool with $30,000/year maintenance over five years costs $200,000 — far more than the sticker price. Overlooking maintenance costs is flagged in the CISSP curriculum as a common executive communication mistake.

Domain 6 Wrap-Up — What the Exam Will Test

Domain 6 is the evidence domain. Its central message: security controls are only worth claiming if you can prove they work. Every topic — from penetration testing to log normalisation to management review — is in service of that proof.

The exam loves to test distinctions in this domain. VA vs pen-test. Black vs white vs gray box. Blind vs double-blind. Type I vs Type II. SOC 1 vs SOC 2 vs SOC 3. KPI vs KRI. Synthetic transactions vs RUM. Suspend vs delete. Make sure you can answer "what is the difference between X and Y?" for every pair in that list — the exam will present a scenario and ask which one applies.

The second major exam theme is communication: writing reports for the right audience, quantifying risk in monetary terms, presenting options to leadership with objective criteria, and running effective management reviews. These soft-skill concepts appear in scenario questions testing whether you understand the full accountability chain from the technical team up to the board.

Finally, remember the PDCA loop. Plan (set goals and scope). Do (run the tests). Check (analyse results — the bulk of Domain 6). Act (management review and implement improvements). Everything in this domain is one step in that loop. If you ever feel lost on a Domain 6 question, ask yourself: "which phase of PDCA is this describing?" — and the answer will point you to the right concept.

Quick-reference exam checklist for Domain 6: VA = identify & rank (no exploitation) | Pen-test = actively exploit (with authorisation) | Black = no knowledge | White = full knowledge | Gray = partial | Blind = public data only, staff notified | Double-blind = staff not notified | CVE = name | CVSS = score 0–10 | SCAP = automation | SOC 1 = financial | SOC 2 = security (CIA+P+A) | SOC 3 = public summary | Type I = design, snapshot | Type II = operating effectiveness, period | KPI = past performance | KRI = future risk exposure | SIEM stages: Generate → Transmit → Collect → Normalise → Analyse → Retain → Dispose | Suspend (don't delete) departed-user accounts | Backups must be restore-tested to be reliable | Life-cycle cost, not sticker price | PDCA: Plan-Do-Check-Act

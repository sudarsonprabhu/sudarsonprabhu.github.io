Domain 5 · 13% average exam weight

Identity and Access Management (IAM)

Controlling who and what can reach an organisation’s assets — how identities are proved, how access is granted and enforced, and how that access is governed across its entire lifecycle.

Back to CISSP Notes

Sub-domains

Exam outline areas.

Domain 5 is broken into six exam outline areas that follow an identity from the moment it is created, through how it proves itself and what it is allowed to do, to how that access is reviewed and eventually removed.

5.1 — Control physical & logical access to assets

Governing access to the six asset types — information, systems, devices, facilities, applications, and services — using both physical controls (locks, guards, mantraps, badges) and logical controls (permissions, ACLs, authentication). The principle: only the right subject reaches the right object, in the right way.

5.2 — Identification & authentication strategy

Designing how people, devices, and services prove who they are — identity proofing and registration, the three factor types, MFA and passwordless, session management, SSO and federated identity, credential vaults, and just-in-time access.

5.3 — Federated identity with a third party

Extending trust across organisational boundaries — on-premise, cloud, and hybrid federation — so an identity issued in one domain is honoured in another (SAML, OIDC, OAuth), removing the need for separate accounts everywhere.

5.4 — Implement & manage authorization

Enforcing what an authenticated subject may do using access-control models — RBAC, rule-based, MAC, DAC, ABAC, and risk-based — together with policy enforcement architecture: policy decision points (PDP) and policy enforcement points (PEP).

5.5 — Identity & access provisioning lifecycle

Running identity end-to-end — provisioning and deprovisioning, periodic account access reviews, role definition and transition, privilege escalation and sudo auditing, and disciplined service-account management.

5.6 — Implement authentication systems

Deploying the protocols that operationalise authentication across the enterprise — Kerberos, RADIUS / TACACS+ / Diameter, SAML, OpenID Connect, and OAuth 2.0 — and knowing where each fits and how each fails.

Domain 5 — Complete Guide

Identity & Access Management

A complete instructor-led walkthrough of every concept tested in CISSP Domain 5, written the way a classroom trainer would teach a beginner — plain language first, then the exam-grade precision. No prior knowledge assumed. Each concept is built from the ground up: what it is, why it exists, how it actually works, and the exact distinction the exam will try to trick you on.

01

Domain Overview

Section 1 — What Domain 5 Is Really About: The IAAA Model

13% Exam Weight

One of the larger domains. IAM concepts also bleed into Domains 1, 3 and 7.

The Core: IAAA

Identification → Authentication → Authorization → Accountability.

Subject & Object

Subject = the active entity (user). Object = the passive resource (file).

Exam Style

Definitions, ordering, and “which factor / which model” selection questions.

Let me start the way I would on the first day of class. Every other domain in the CISSP is, in some sense, about protecting things — networks, software, data centres, cryptographic keys. Domain 5 is about protecting access to those things. It answers one deceptively simple question that sits underneath the entire security profession: “Are you allowed to be here, and how do I know?” Get this domain right and you understand the gatekeeping logic that every system on earth depends on. Get it wrong and an attacker simply walks in the front door wearing a borrowed badge.

Before we touch a single technology, you must internalise two words that the exam uses constantly: subject and object. A subject is the active entity that requests access — usually a person, but it can also be a process, a service, or a device acting on someone’s behalf. An object is the passive resource being accessed — a file, a database row, a printer, a door. Access control, at its heart, is simply the set of rules that governs how subjects are permitted to interact with objects. Whenever a CISSP question feels abstract, re-read it and label the subject and the object; the fog usually clears immediately.

Now the spine of the whole domain: a four-step model the industry calls IAAA — Identification, Authentication, Authorization, and Accountability (some texts add a fifth A for Auditing, which is really the mechanism behind Accountability). These four steps always happen in this exact order, and the exam loves to test whether you know which step is which. Think of walking into a secure office building. First you claim who you are by stating your name at reception — that is Identification. Then you prove it by showing your photo ID — that is Authentication. The guard then checks a list to decide which floors your badge may open — that is Authorization. And finally, the building logs every door your badge touches so that later, if something goes wrong, your movements can be reconstructed — that is Accountability.

Hold onto the difference between the first two steps, because beginners confuse them more than anything else in this domain. Identification is a claim; authentication is the proof of that claim. Typing your username is identification — anyone can type your username, it is not a secret. Typing your password is authentication — that is the part only you should be able to do. A username uniquely identifies you in the system; a password (or fingerprint, or token) is what verifies that the person typing the username really is you. If you remember nothing else from this section, remember that identification asserts an identity and authentication validates it.

Accountability is the step people forget, but it is the reason audit logs exist. Accountability means that every action on a system can be traced back to a unique, authenticated individual — it makes non-repudiation possible, the property that a user cannot credibly deny having performed an action. This is precisely why shared accounts are a cardinal sin in security: if five administrators all log in as “admin”, the logs can prove that “admin” deleted the database, but they can never prove which human did it. No individual accountability, no non-repudiation. The exam will reward you every time for choosing “assign unique individual accounts” over any answer that tolerates shared credentials.

🌟 Exam Insight

Memorise the IAAA order and the one-word essence of each: Identification = claim, Authentication = prove, Authorization = permit, Accountability = trace. The classic trap: a question describes typing a username and asks what just happened — the answer is identification, not authentication. And remember the golden rule of accountability: it requires unique identities, which is why shared/generic accounts destroy non-repudiation.

5.1 — Controlling Access to Assets

Section 2 — Physical and Logical Controls Over the Six Asset Types

Six Asset Types

Information, Systems, Devices, Facilities, Applications, Services.

Physical Controls

Locks, guards, fences, mantraps, badges, bollards, lighting.

Logical / Technical

Permissions, ACLs, authentication, encryption, firewalls.

Administrative

Policies, procedures, training, background checks, separation of duties.

Sub-domain 5.1 asks you to control access to assets, and the exam outline names six categories you should be able to recite: information (the data itself), systems (servers and the platforms that run workloads), devices (laptops, phones, removable media), facilities (buildings, data centres, wiring closets), applications (the software users interact with), and services (APIs, cloud services, and the functions other systems consume). The reason this list matters is that a complete access-control programme must address all six — it is no good hardening your servers if anyone can walk into the data centre, and no good locking the data centre if your web application leaks records to the internet.

Controls themselves come in two great families that you must never blur: physical and logical (the latter often called technical). Physical controls operate in the tangible world — they stop a body from reaching an asset. Think of locked doors, security guards, fences, bollards, lighting, security dogs, and the famous mantrap (a small two-door vestibule where the second door will not open until the first has closed and locked, defeating tailgating, where an unauthorised person slips in behind an authorised one). Logical controls operate inside the machine — they stop a digital request from reaching a digital resource. Think of passwords, permissions, access control lists, encryption, firewalls, and intrusion prevention. The same conceptual goal — keep the wrong subject away from the object — is enforced once in atoms and once in bits.

There is a third family the exam weaves in here: administrative (or managerial) controls — the policies, procedures, training, background checks, and the human-process rules such as separation of duties and mandatory vacations. The big-picture model the CISSP wants you to hold is that strong security layers all three: administrative controls set the rules, physical controls enforce them in the building, and technical controls enforce them in the systems. This layering is the essence of defence in depth — no single control is trusted to be perfect, so controls are stacked such that an attacker who defeats one still faces the next.

When you operate on objects, the exam frequently borrows the database vocabulary of CRUD — Create, Read, Update, Delete — as the canonical set of operations a subject might be permitted to perform. Authorization, which we cover in depth later, is really about deciding which of these verbs each subject may apply to each object. A junior clerk might be permitted to read a record but not delete it; an auditor might be permitted to read everything and write nothing at all. Keep CRUD in mind: it is the granular language of permissions.

One asset-access technology that appears in exam scenarios is RFID (Radio Frequency Identification) — a system where physical objects carry electronic tags that communicate wirelessly with a reader, which then queries a back-end database. The three components are the RFID tag (passive, no battery, powered by the reader’s field), the RFID reader, and the back-end database. RFID is the best choice for automated inventory control because passive tags require no line-of-sight, are cost-effective at scale, and require no manual scanning. The security weakness is that data can be intercepted between the tag and reader; the countermeasure is encryption, though this is limited because passive RFID tags have very low processing power and cannot run heavy cryptographic algorithms. For the exam: RFID = inventory automation; encryption = the countermeasure; processing power limitation = why strong encryption is difficult to implement on passive tags.

🌟 Exam Insight

Know the three control types by implementation — physical (tangible: locks, guards, mantraps), technical/logical (systems: passwords, ACLs, encryption), administrative (people/process: policy, training). A mantrap is a physical, preventive control that defeats tailgating/piggybacking. For RFID: best for automated inventory tracking; security risk = interception between tag and reader; countermeasure = encryption, limited by low processing power of passive tags.

5.2 — Identification

Section 3 — Identification, Registration, and Identity Proofing

Identity Proofing

Verifying a person is who they claim before issuing credentials.

Registration

Enrolling the verified identity and binding it to credentials.

IAL 1/2/3

NIST Identity Assurance Levels — rising rigour of proofing.

Uniqueness

Every identifier must map to exactly one subject — no sharing.

We said identification is the act of claiming an identity, but a claim is only meaningful if, at some earlier point, the organisation actually confirmed that the person behind the identity is real. That earlier point is called identity proofing, and it is one of the most under-appreciated steps in the whole domain. Before a bank ever issues you online-banking credentials, it verifies your real-world identity — checking a passport or driving licence, perhaps asking knowledge-based questions only you should be able to answer, perhaps a video call. Identity proofing is the foundation everything else stands on: if you authenticate a credential that was issued to an impostor, every subsequent control is faithfully protecting the wrong person.

Once a person is proofed, they go through registration (also called enrolment), where the verified identity is recorded in the system and bound to one or more credentials — a username paired with a password, a smart card, an enrolled fingerprint. This binding is the moment a real human becomes a digital subject. The quality of this whole process is graded by NIST SP 800-63 into Identity Assurance Levels (IAL): IAL1 requires no real-world proofing (self-asserted, like signing up for a free newsletter), IAL2 requires evidence that the claimed identity is real and belongs to the person (remote or in-person verification of documents), and IAL3 requires in-person or supervised verification by a trained agent with physical document inspection. The higher the stakes, the higher the IAL you demand.

The non-negotiable property of any identifier is uniqueness. Every username, employee ID, or account must map to exactly one subject — we are back to the accountability principle from Section 1. Identifiers should also generally be non-descriptive and not reveal sensitive information: a username like “admin” or “ceo” advertises a high-value target, and an identifier that embeds a national ID number leaks data. Good practice issues identifiers that are unique, consistent, and reveal as little as possible about the privilege or the person behind them.

🌟 Exam Insight

Distinguish the three lifecycle moments: proofing (verify the human is real, before issuance) → registration/enrolment (bind the identity to credentials) → identification (claim the identity at login). The exam test of judgement: increasing identity assurance means strengthening proofing, not strengthening the password. Higher-risk systems demand higher IAL.

5.2 — Authentication Factors

Section 4 — The Three Authentication Factors

Type 1 — Knowledge

Something you know: password, PIN, passphrase, security question.

Type 2 — Ownership

Something you have: token, smart card, phone, OTP device.

Type 3 — Biometric

Something you are: fingerprint, iris, face, voice, gait.

Two more (context)

Somewhere you are (location) · Something you do (behaviour).

Authentication is where the domain gets its richest detail, and it all organises around three factor types that you must know cold, including their “Type” numbers because the exam uses them as shorthand. Type 1, something you know — knowledge in your head, like a password, a PIN, a passphrase, or the answer to a security question. Type 2, something you have — a physical possession, like a hardware token, a smart card, or your phone receiving a one-time code. Type 3, something you are — a physical or behavioural characteristic of your body, like a fingerprint, an iris pattern, or your voice. Two further factors appear in modern texts as context: somewhere you are (geolocation, IP, GPS) and something you do (behavioural patterns such as how you type or swipe).

Each factor type has a characteristic strength and a characteristic weakness, and understanding the trade-off is what the exam actually tests. Knowledge factors are cheap and easy to deploy but easy to steal — they can be guessed, phished, shoulder-surfed, or cracked, and crucially a stolen password can be used remotely by anyone with no further obstacle. Ownership factors are stronger because an attacker must physically obtain the token, but tokens can be lost, stolen, or cloned, and the user is inconvenienced when they leave it at home. Biometric factors are the hardest to forge and impossible to forget, but they are expensive, raise privacy concerns, and carry a unique problem: you cannot reset your fingerprint — if biometric template data is breached, the user is compromised for life.

This is the precise reason we combine factors into multi-factor authentication, which we cover fully in Section 8. But here is the single most important definitional point in the entire authentication topic, and the exam tests it relentlessly: multi-factor means factors of different TYPES, not multiple instances of the same type. A password plus a security question is not multi-factor — both are Type 1, something you know, so a single phishing attack harvests both. A password plus a code from your phone is multi-factor — one is Type 1, the other is Type 2, so the attacker must both steal your password and physically possess your phone. The whole value of MFA comes from forcing an attacker to defeat two fundamentally different kinds of obstacle at once.

🌟 Exam Insight

The most-tested trap in this domain: a scenario lists two credentials and asks if it is multi-factor. Count the types, not the credentials. Password + PIN + security question = still single-factor (all Type 1). Password + smartphone OTP = two-factor. True MFA crosses factor categories. Also remember: biometrics are the only factor you cannot change after a breach.

5.2 — Knowledge Factors

Section 5 — Passwords, Passphrases, and Why Knowledge Is the Weakest Factor

Passphrase > Password

Length beats complexity. A long phrase resists brute force better.

Cognitive Q&A

Security questions — weak; answers are often public/guessable.

Salt + Hash

Never store plaintext. Salt defeats rainbow tables.

NIST 800-63B

Favour length, screen against breached lists, drop forced rotation.

Knowledge factors are the oldest and most common form of authentication, and also the weakest, so the exam expects you to understand both how they work and how they fail. A password is a secret string; a PIN is a short numeric password; a passphrase is a longer sequence of words that is easier for a human to remember and far harder for a machine to brute-force because its strength comes from length rather than from a few substituted symbols. The modern guidance — reflected in NIST SP 800-63B and very testable — is that a long passphrase such as “correct-horse-battery-staple” is stronger and more usable than a short, tortured password like “P@ssw0rd!”. Length is the dominant lever for resisting brute force.

A weaker cousin is the cognitive password, better known as the security question — “What was your first pet’s name?” These are convenient for account recovery but genuinely insecure, because the answers are often discoverable on social media, guessable, or shared across many sites. Treat them as a low-assurance fallback, never as a serious second factor. Remember from Section 4: a password plus a security question is still single-factor, because both live in your memory as Type 1 knowledge. An important nuance the exam tests: authorising the request for a new user account happens before account creation, but that authorisation step is not a standard element of user account administration — it is a separate approval step. User account administration covers establishing, issuing, and closing accounts; tracking access; and periodic verification. Authorising the request is a prerequisite, not part of the administration cycle itself.

How a password is stored matters as much as how strong it is, and this is a favourite exam theme. Systems must never store passwords in plaintext. Instead they store a hash — a one-way cryptographic fingerprint that cannot be reversed back into the password. When you log in, the system hashes what you typed and compares it to the stored hash. To defeat precomputed attack tables (rainbow tables), each password is combined with a unique random value called a salt before hashing, so that two users with the same password produce completely different stored hashes. Modern systems go further and use deliberately slow, memory-hard hashing functions (bcrypt, scrypt, Argon2, PBKDF2) so that even a stolen hash database is painfully expensive to crack.

You must also be able to name the attacks. A brute-force attack tries every possible combination. A dictionary attack tries lists of likely words and known-common passwords. A hybrid attack mutates dictionary words with numbers and symbols. Credential stuffing replays username/password pairs stolen from one breached site against many other sites, exploiting password reuse. Password spraying flips the logic — it tries one or two very common passwords against many accounts to stay under lockout thresholds. The countermeasures are equally testable: enforce account lockout after a few failed attempts to stop online guessing, screen new passwords against breached-password lists, and crucially — per current NIST guidance — stop forcing arbitrary periodic password rotation, because mandatory frequent changes drive users toward weak, predictable patterns. Rotate only on evidence of compromise.

🌟 Exam Insight

Modern NIST 800-63B positions: favour length over forced complexity, screen against breach lists, allow long passphrases and paste, and do not mandate periodic rotation without cause — this reverses older “change every 90 days” dogma, and the exam now tests the new view. Storage answer is always salted hash with a slow function (bcrypt/Argon2), never encryption and never plaintext. Salt specifically defeats rainbow tables.

5.2 — Ownership Factors

Section 6 — Tokens, One-Time Passwords, and Smart Cards

Synchronous OTP

Time-based (TOTP) — code changes every ~30s, clock-synced.

Asynchronous OTP

Counter / challenge-response (HOTP) — event-based.

Hard vs Soft Token

Hard = physical device; Soft = app (Google/Microsoft Authenticator).

Smart vs Memory Card

Smart card has a processor; memory card only stores data.

Ownership factors prove possession of a physical thing, and the workhorse of this category is the one-time password (OTP) — a code that is valid for a single login and then useless, which defeats replay attacks because a stolen code cannot be reused. OTPs come in two flavours, and distinguishing them is a guaranteed exam point. Synchronous tokens are usually time-based: the token and the server share a secret seed and both run the same clock, so they independently compute the same code every interval (typically 30 seconds). This is the TOTP algorithm (Time-based One-Time Password) that powers Google Authenticator and most modern apps. Asynchronous tokens are event/counter-based or challenge-response: the code advances each time you press a button (the HOTP algorithm, HMAC-based One-Time Password using a counter), or the server issues a random challenge that the token must transform into a correct response. A simple memory hook: synchronous = synchronised clocks (time); asynchronous = a counter or a challenge.

Tokens also split by physical form. A hard token is a dedicated physical device — the classic RSA SecurID key fob with its rolling six-digit display, or a USB security key. A soft token is software that turns a general-purpose device into a token, like an authenticator app on your phone. Hard tokens are harder to compromise remotely because malware on your laptop cannot reach into a separate device, but they cost money and get lost; soft tokens are free and convenient but inherit the security of the phone they run on. Both are legitimately Type 2 because possession of the device is what is being proven.

Then there are cards, and the exam loves the distinction between two that sound alike. A memory card only stores data — think of an old hotel key card or a magnetic-stripe card; it has no processing ability and is easily copied. A smart card contains an embedded microprocessor chip that can perform cryptographic operations internally, so secrets never leave the chip — this is your modern chip-and-PIN bank card or a government PIV/CAC card. Because a smart card can compute, it can prove possession without exposing its secret key, making it dramatically more secure than a memory card. The one-line discriminator: smart card processes, memory card only stores.

One honest caveat the exam increasingly reflects: OTPs delivered by SMS text message are the weakest form of second factor, vulnerable to SIM-swapping and interception, and NIST has deprecated SMS for high-assurance use. App-based TOTP is better; hardware security keys using FIDO2/WebAuthn (which also resist phishing because they are cryptographically bound to the legitimate site) are best. When a question contrasts second-factor methods, the security ranking is roughly: hardware key > authenticator app (TOTP) > push notification > SMS.

🌟 Exam Insight

Lock these pairs: Synchronous = time-based (TOTP); Asynchronous = counter/event or challenge-response (HOTP). Hard token = physical device; soft token = app. Smart card = on-board processor; memory card = storage only. And the modern ranking: SMS OTP is the weakest 2FA (SIM swap), hardware FIDO2 keys are phishing-resistant and strongest.

5.2 — Biometric Factors

Section 7 — Biometrics: Accuracy, FAR, FRR, and the Crossover Error Rate

Type I = FRR

False Rejection — a legitimate user is denied. Hurts usability.

Type II = FAR

False Acceptance — an impostor is admitted. The security risk.

CER / EER

Where FRR = FAR. Lower CER = more accurate device.

Physiological vs Behavioural

Who you are (fingerprint) vs how you act (voice, gait).

Biometrics authenticate you by measuring something about your body, and they divide into two groups. Physiological biometrics measure static physical traits: fingerprint, hand geometry, vascular (vein) pattern, facial structure, iris, and retina. Behavioural biometrics measure how you do something: your voice, signature dynamics, keystroke rhythm, and gait (the way you walk). A quick rule of thumb — physiological is “what you are”, behavioural is “what you do”, but both are still classed as Type 3. Of the physiological methods, iris scanning is generally regarded as the most accurate and stable over a lifetime, while retina scanning is highly accurate but intrusive (it requires a close eye scan and can reveal medical conditions, raising privacy concerns). When a biometric is enrolled, the system does not store a raw image; it extracts and stores a mathematical template, which is what makes a biometric database breach so serious — remember, you cannot reissue a fingerprint.

Now the part the exam tests more than any other biometric concept: accuracy errors. No biometric is perfect, so every system makes two kinds of mistake. A False Rejection, also called a Type I error, happens when a legitimate user is wrongly denied — the system fails to recognise its own authorised person. The rate at which this happens is the False Rejection Rate (FRR). A False Acceptance, also called a Type II error, happens when an impostor is wrongly accepted — the system mistakes a stranger for an authorised user. The rate is the False Acceptance Rate (FAR). Keep them straight with this logic: rejection annoys the right people (an availability and usability problem), while acceptance admits the wrong people (a genuine security breach). For that reason, in high-security environments you tune the sensitivity to minimise FAR even if it means a higher FRR — better to occasionally re-scan a legitimate employee than to ever let an impostor through.

FAR and FRR move in opposite directions as you adjust the device’s sensitivity. Crank sensitivity up and you reject more impostors but also more legitimate users (FAR falls, FRR rises); loosen it and you admit legitimate users more easily but also more impostors (FRR falls, FAR rises). Plot both curves against sensitivity and they cross at a single point. That crossing point is the Crossover Error Rate (CER), also called the Equal Error Rate (EER) — the sensitivity setting at which FRR equals FAR. The CER is the headline figure for comparing biometric devices: the lower the CER, the more accurate the device overall. If device A has a CER of 1% and device B has a CER of 3%, device A is the better biometric, full stop. This single fact — lower CER is better — is one of the highest-yield exam points in Domain 5.

Two more practical biometric concepts round this out. Enrolment is the one-time process of registering your biometric and building the template; if it takes too long or fails too often, the system has poor usability. Throughput rate is the time required to scan and approve or deny each person — how quickly the system processes individuals in a queue — and it directly determines whether a biometric is practical in a high-volume environment like a data-centre entrance with hundreds of daily badge-ins. And then there is user acceptance: this is the most common difficulty with biometric systems, and the exam tests this exact point. Accuracy issues and cost matter, but when a question asks what the biggest real-world obstacle to biometric deployment is, the answer is gaining user acceptance. People resist having their fingerprints, irises, or faces scanned — for privacy reasons, cultural reasons, or simply discomfort — and a technically superb biometric that employees refuse to enrol in is a failed control. Design a biometric rollout and user acceptance must be your first concern, before you even consider accuracy metrics.

🌟 Exam Insight

Burn this in: Type I error = False Rejection (FRR) = legitimate user denied; Type II error = False Acceptance (FAR) = impostor admitted — Type II is the dangerous one. They are inversely related as sensitivity changes, and they meet at the Crossover Error Rate (CER/EER). To compare two biometric devices, pick the one with the lower CER. In high-security settings, bias toward a low FAR even at the cost of higher FRR. The two most accurate biometric systems are iris scans (most accurate, hardest to counterfeit, stable over a lifetime) and vascular pattern scans (unique vein patterns). When sensitivity increases: FRR increases and FAR decreases. The most common difficulty with biometric deployment is gaining user acceptance — not accuracy, not cost.

5.2 — MFA, Passwordless & Sessions

Section 8 — Multi-Factor, Passwordless, Assurance Levels and Session Management

MFA

Two or more different factor types — the single biggest account-takeover defence.

Passwordless

FIDO2 / WebAuthn, passkeys, biometrics — no shared secret to phish.

AAL 1/2/3

NIST Authenticator Assurance Levels — AAL3 needs a hardware crypto authenticator.

JIT Access

Just-in-time: grant elevated rights only for the moment they are needed.

We have met the three factors; now we combine and govern them. Multi-factor authentication (MFA) requires two or more factors of different types, and it is, empirically, the most effective single control against account takeover — even a phished password is useless to an attacker who lacks the second factor. The exam will reward MFA as the answer to a huge range of “how do we stop credential theft” scenarios. Be careful, though, with the closely related term two-step verification: a password followed by an emailed code is two steps but, if both arrive through things-you-know channels, may not be two true factors. Always re-check that the steps cross factor types.

Passwordless authentication is the modern direction of travel: it removes the shared secret entirely. Instead of a password, the user authenticates with a cryptographic key stored on a device (FIDO2/WebAuthn, increasingly marketed as passkeys) often unlocked by a local biometric or PIN. Because there is no password to phish, reuse, or steal from a server, passwordless methods eliminate whole categories of attack — phishing, credential stuffing, and database password breaches all become moot. The device proves possession of a private key that never leaves it, and the matching public key is all the server stores. When a question asks for the most phishing-resistant approach, FIDO2-based passwordless is the modern best answer.

NIST grades authentication strength with Authenticator Assurance Levels (AAL), the authentication-side counterpart to the IAL we met in Section 3. AAL1 permits single-factor authentication (e.g. a password alone) for low-risk access. AAL2 requires multi-factor authentication, proving possession and control of two distinct factors. AAL3 is the highest level: it requires a hardware-based cryptographic authenticator that is verifier-impersonation-resistant (phishing-resistant) — think a FIDO2 hardware key or PIV smart card. The pattern to remember: the more sensitive the system, the higher the AAL you mandate, and AAL3 specifically demands hardware crypto, not just an app code.

Modern IAM also embraces just-in-time (JIT) access: rather than granting standing elevated privileges that sit dormant and dangerous, the system grants the elevated right only at the moment it is needed and revokes it immediately after — shrinking the window an attacker can exploit. JIT is a pillar of Zero Trust because it verifies every access request and grants only the minimum necessary access for that specific moment. The exam recognises three distinct forms: JIT Identity (a temporary user account provisioned on demand and removed after the task), JIT Privileged Access (admin-level rights granted for a specific operation, then immediately revoked), and Ephemeral accounts (accounts created for a single session and immediately deleted after use — the most restrictive form, since nothing persists to compromise). All three reduce insider threats, limit privilege abuse, and prevent lateral movement because standing privileges simply do not exist. JIT pairs naturally with Privileged Access Management (PAM). Also know context-aware authentication: it evaluates location, time of day, and device type — especially in MDM environments — and demands step-up authentication when those signals indicate elevated risk. Finally, none of this matters if the session established after login is left unguarded. Good session management means binding the session to a strong, random, unpredictable session identifier; enforcing idle timeouts (log the user out after inactivity) and absolute timeouts (cap the total session length); regenerating the session ID after privilege changes; and invalidating the session on logout. We meet the attack this defends against — session hijacking — in Section 15, but the design principle belongs here: authentication proves who you are once, while session management keeps that proof from being stolen for the rest of your visit.

🌟 Exam Insight

Map the two NIST ladders: IAL grades identity proofing, AAL grades authentication strength (AAL2 = MFA, AAL3 = hardware crypto authenticator). MFA is the go-to answer for stopping account takeover; FIDO2/passwordless is the most phishing-resistant. And remember session controls: short idle timeout + absolute timeout + a strong random session ID regenerated after login.

5.4 — Authorization Models

Section 9 — DAC, MAC, RBAC, Rule-Based, ABAC and Risk-Based Access Control

DAC

Owner decides. Flexible, used in commercial OSes. Identity-based.

MAC

System enforces labels (Top Secret). Rigid, used in military.

RBAC

Access by job role. Scales to large enterprises.

ABAC

Access by attributes & policy. Most granular & dynamic.

Once a subject is authenticated, authorization decides what that subject may actually do, and the CISSP expects fluency in the major access-control models. The first is Discretionary Access Control (DAC), where the owner of a resource decides who may access it — access is at the owner’s discretion. When you right-click a file and share it with a colleague, that is DAC in action. It is flexible and intuitive, which is why it underpins commercial operating systems like Windows and Linux file permissions, but that same flexibility is its weakness: an owner can carelessly or maliciously over-share, and DAC is therefore vulnerable to malware running with a user’s rights and to the spread of access through Trojan horses. DAC is identity-based — the access control list names the specific users or groups allowed.

At the opposite pole is Mandatory Access Control (MAC), where the system, not the owner, enforces access based on security labels. Every object carries a classification (Unclassified, Confidential, Secret, Top Secret) and every subject carries a clearance, and the operating system mechanically enforces the rules — no owner can override them. MAC is rigid, expensive, and used where confidentiality is paramount: military and government systems, and specialised secure OSes like SELinux. The exam association is fixed: MAC = labels, clearances, lattice, military/government, non-discretionary and rigid. Because the rules are imposed top-down rather than left to owners, MAC is the textbook example of non-discretionary access control.

Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) is the model that actually runs most large enterprises, because it solves a scaling problem. Instead of assigning permissions to individuals one by one, you define roles that mirror job functions — “Accounts Payable Clerk”, “Help-Desk Tier 1”, “Database Administrator” — attach the appropriate permissions to each role, and then simply assign people to roles. When someone changes jobs, you move them to a new role and their access realigns automatically; when a thousand new hires arrive, you drop them into existing roles rather than hand-crafting a thousand permission sets. RBAC is the natural enforcement engine for least privilege at scale. One nuance the exam tests: RBAC is technically a form of DAC, because the roles themselves are determined by data owners and managers per company policy — the owner ultimately decides who belongs to which role. This surprises many students but it is the authoritative CISSP position: RBAC falls under the DAC family because discretionary decisions are made at the role-definition level, even though individual users do not directly set permissions. RBAC is most efficient when assignments to data permissions for user groups change frequently. Do not confuse it with Rule-Based Access Control (sometimes also abbreviated RBAC, which is why context matters): rule-based control applies global if/then rules to everyone regardless of identity — a firewall ruleset is the classic example, as is “no one may log in outside business hours.” Rule-based is most commonly a form of DAC as well, since system owners create the rules.

The most modern and granular model is Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC), which makes decisions by evaluating four categories of attributes against a written policy: subject attributes (age, clearance, role, department), object attributes (type, classification, sensitivity), action attributes (read, write, delete — what the subject wants to do), and contextual/environment attributes (time of day, location, device health). ABAC can express rich, dynamic rules such as “permit a finance-department employee to read salary records only from a managed device, during business hours, from inside the corporate network.” ABAC policies are written in a structured language called XACML (eXtensible Access Control Markup Language) — a point the exam tests directly. ABAC enforces elements of both DAC and MAC and is the foundation of zero-trust and modern policy-based cloud authorization. A close relative is risk-based (adaptive) access control, which evaluates the risk of each request in real time — an impossible-travel login or an unrecognised device raises the risk score and triggers step-up authentication or denial. Where ABAC asks “do the attributes satisfy policy?”, risk-based control asks “how risky does this specific attempt look right now?”

🌟 Exam Insight

Match each model to its trigger words: DAC = owner’s discretion, identity-based, flexible, commercial OS. MAC = labels/clearances, non-discretionary, rigid, military. RBAC = job roles, scales for enterprises — and RBAC is a form of DAC (roles set by owners/managers). Rule-based = global if/then rules (firewalls, time-of-day) applied to all. ABAC = subject/object/action/environment attributes + policy, most granular, powers zero-trust; policy language = XACML. Risk-based = real-time adaptive scoring with step-up auth. ABAC is best for complex contextual rules like “salespeople from managed devices on corporate wireless between 8am–6pm.”

5.4 — Enforcement Architecture

Section 10 — Access Control Matrix, ACLs, Capabilities, and the PDP/PEP

Access Control Matrix

Master table of subjects (rows) × objects (columns).

ACL = column

Per-object list: who can touch this file.

Capability = row

Per-subject list: what this user can touch.

PDP / PEP

PDP decides; PEP enforces at the gate.

Models describe the philosophy of authorization; mechanisms are how that philosophy is actually represented and enforced inside a system. The conceptual master structure is the access control matrix — a giant table with subjects as rows and objects as columns, where each cell records the permissions that subject holds over that object. Real systems rarely store the full matrix (it would be enormous and mostly empty), so they slice it two ways, and the exam wants you to know which slice is which. Reading the matrix column by column gives you an Access Control List (ACL): attached to each object, it lists every subject and what they may do to it — “this file: Alice can read/write, Bob can read.” Reading the matrix row by row gives you a capability table: attached to each subject, it lists every object that subject may reach — “Alice: read/write file1, read file2.” The crisp distinction: ACL is object-centric (a column), capability is subject-centric (a row).

In a modern, policy-driven environment — especially ABAC and zero-trust — authorization is split into clearly named architectural roles that the exam increasingly references. The Policy Enforcement Point (PEP) is the gate that actually sits in the request path and lets traffic through or blocks it; it does not make the decision itself, it asks. The Policy Decision Point (PDP) is the brain that evaluates the applicable policy and returns a permit/deny verdict. Supporting them, the Policy Administration Point (PAP) is where policies are authored and managed, and the Policy Information Point (PIP) supplies the attribute data (user department, device health, time) the PDP needs to decide. The flow is: a subject hits the PEP, the PEP forwards the request to the PDP, the PDP pulls attributes from the PIP, evaluates the policy from the PAP, and returns a decision the PEP then enforces. Remember the two-word essence: PDP decides, PEP enforces.

Two more enforcement ideas deserve a mention because they show up in scenario questions. A constrained user interface limits what a subject can even attempt — greying out menu options, hiding records, or presenting a restricted view — so that unauthorised actions are never offered in the first place (database views and restricted shells are examples). And content-dependent versus context-dependent control: content-dependent decisions hinge on the data inside the object (you may see the employee table but the salary column is masked), while context-dependent decisions hinge on the circumstances of the request (the sequence of prior actions, the time, the location). These granular controls are how the abstract models of Section 9 become concrete enforcement.

🌟 Exam Insight

The classic mechanism trap: ACL = bound to the object (column of the matrix); capability table = bound to the subject (row). For policy architecture, memorise PDP decides / PEP enforces, with the PIP feeding attributes and the PAP holding the policies. Content-dependent = based on the data; context-dependent = based on the situation/sequence.

5.2 / 5.6 — SSO & Kerberos

Section 11 — Single Sign-On and the Kerberos Protocol in Depth

SSO Trade-off

One login for many systems — convenient, but a single point of failure.

KDC

Key Distribution Center = the trusted core (AS + TGS).

TGT

Ticket-Granting Ticket — your proof you already logged in.

Tickets, not passwords

Kerberos uses symmetric keys + time-stamped tickets; needs synced clocks.

Single Sign-On (SSO) lets a user authenticate once and then reach many systems without logging in again. The benefit is obvious — fewer passwords to remember and reuse, less helpdesk burden, a smoother experience. But the exam always wants you to name the trade-off: SSO concentrates risk into a single point of failure. If an attacker captures that one set of credentials, they inherit access to everything the user could reach. That is precisely why SSO must be paired with strong, ideally multi-factor, authentication at the front door — the stronger the single sign-on, the more it must be protected.

The canonical SSO protocol for a single organisation — and the one CISSP tests in real detail — is Kerberos, the authentication system at the heart of Microsoft Active Directory. Learn its vocabulary first. The KDC (Key Distribution Center) is the trusted central server and the brain of the system; it contains two services: the Authentication Service (AS), which verifies who you are at initial login, and the Ticket-Granting Service (TGS), which issues tickets for specific resources afterward. A principal is any entity Kerberos knows (a user or a service). A ticket is a time-stamped, encrypted token that proves you have authenticated, and the most important of these is the Ticket-Granting Ticket (TGT) — think of it as a wristband you receive at the door proving you have already been verified, so you do not have to show your ID again at every counter inside.

Here is the flow, told as a story. You log in, and your client sends a request to the AS. The AS verifies you and returns a TGT encrypted with a key only the KDC knows, plus a session key — crucially, your password itself is never sent across the network. Later, when you want to reach a specific service (say a file server), your client presents the TGT to the TGS and says “I’ve already been verified, please give me a ticket for the file server.” The TGS validates your TGT and issues a service ticket for that specific server. Your client then presents that service ticket directly to the file server, which trusts it because it is encrypted with a key the server shares with the KDC — and you are granted access. Every ticket is time-stamped and short-lived, which is why Kerberos absolutely depends on synchronised clocks: if a client’s clock drifts more than a few minutes (typically five) from the KDC’s, tickets are rejected as expired or invalid. Time synchronisation (usually via NTP) is therefore a hard requirement, and “clock skew” is a favourite Kerberos exam answer.

Kerberos was designed around four core requirements that are occasionally tested by name: Security (credentials never cross the network in plaintext), Reliability (it must always be available, hence redundant KDCs), Transparency (users experience SSO without seeing the ticket machinery), and Scalability (it must work for thousands of users and services). Know these four properties as the design goals Kerberos was built to satisfy.

Know the weaknesses too. Because the KDC holds all the keys and issues all the tickets, it is a single point of failure — if it goes down, no one can authenticate, so KDCs are deployed redundantly. It is also a high-value target: compromise the KDC and you compromise the realm. Kerberos is also vulnerable to password guessing because the KDC cannot detect dictionary attacks against the initial authentication. Short key lengths increase brute-force risk. And all clocks must be synchronised — a clock drift of more than the permitted skew (typically five minutes) causes ticket rejection. Kerberos-specific attacks the exam tests include: Pass-the-Ticket (steal and reuse a valid Kerberos ticket from memory), Kerberoasting (request service tickets for service accounts and crack the encrypted TGS tickets offline to recover service-account passwords), the devastating Golden Ticket (forge a TGT using the KDC’s KRBTGT account hash — grants complete domain control because a forged TGT bypasses the AS entirely), and the Silver Ticket (forge a Service Ticket using a service account’s hash — grants access to that specific service only, no domain-wide control; less dangerous than Golden but harder to detect since it never touches the KDC). The key distinguisher: Golden Ticket forges a TGT = domain control; Silver Ticket forges a Service Ticket = single service access only. For contrast, SESAME is a European project that extended Kerberos to address some of its limitations — notably it added public-key (asymmetric) cryptography and used Privilege Attribute Certificates (PACs) — whereas classic Kerberos relies on symmetric cryptography throughout. If a question contrasts the two, the headline is: Kerberos = symmetric only; SESAME added asymmetric/public-key support.

🌟 Exam Insight

Kerberos essentials: KDC = AS + TGS; login gets you a TGT from the AS; the TGS exchanges your TGT for a Service Ticket; the password never crosses the wire. Four design goals: Security, Reliability, Transparency, Scalability. Attacks to know: Golden Ticket = forged TGT (domain control, uses KRBTGT hash); Silver Ticket = forged Service Ticket (single service only); Kerberoasting = offline crack of TGS tickets; Pass-the-Ticket = reuse a stolen live ticket. Kerberos needs synchronised clocks (clock skew rejects tickets) and the KDC is a single point of failure. Kerberos is symmetric; SESAME adds asymmetric crypto and PACs.

5.3 / 5.6 — Federated Identity

Section 12 — Federation: SAML, OAuth 2.0, OpenID Connect and WS-Federation

IdP vs SP

Identity Provider asserts; Service Provider (relying party) trusts.

SAML

XML assertions. Enterprise web SSO. AuthN + AuthZ.

OAuth 2.0

Delegated authorization (access), not authentication. Tokens.

OIDC

Authentication layer on top of OAuth 2.0. JSON/JWT, mobile-friendly.

SSO works beautifully inside one organisation, but what happens when you want to log in to a partner’s application, or sign in to a third-party site using your Google account? That is federated identity management (FIM) — extending trust across organisational boundaries so an identity proven in one domain is honoured in another, without the second organisation ever holding your password. The whole thing rests on a trust relationship between two roles you must name precisely. The Identity Provider (IdP) is the party that authenticates the user and asserts their identity (Google, your corporate Azure AD, Okta). The Service Provider (SP), also called the relying party (RP), is the application the user wants to reach; it relies on the IdP’s assertion and trusts it rather than authenticating the user itself. The principal is the user in the middle. Federation, in one sentence: the SP outsources authentication to a trusted IdP.

SAML (Security Assertion Markup Language) is the long-standing enterprise standard for browser-based web SSO and federation. It is XML-based, and its core artefact is the assertion — a signed XML statement from the IdP that conveys three kinds of information: authentication assertions (the user proved who they are), attribute assertions (here are facts about the user, like department or email), and authorization decision assertions (whether the user may access a resource). SAML’s building blocks — which appear in Layout 2 above — are assertions (the statements), protocols (request/response rules), bindings (how messages ride on transport like HTTP POST or redirect), and profiles (combinations for specific use cases, most famously the Web Browser SSO profile). When you log in to a corporate SaaS app and get redirected to your company’s login page and back, that is almost certainly SAML at work.

Now the distinction the exam tests most ruthlessly in this section: OAuth versus OpenID Connect. OAuth 2.0 is a framework for delegated authorization, not authentication — its job is to let you grant one application limited access to your data on another without sharing your password. The classic example: a photo-printing site asks for permission to read your Google Photos; you approve, and Google issues the printing site an access token scoped to just that permission. OAuth answers “what is this app allowed to do on my behalf?” It says nothing, by itself, about who you are. Four key roles in OAuth: Resource owner (the user), Client (the requesting app), Authorization server (issues tokens), and Resource server (holds the protected data). OpenID Connect (OIDC) fixes the identity gap by adding a thin authentication layer on top of OAuth 2.0: it introduces the ID token — a signed JWT (JSON Web Token) that actually proves the user’s identity. So the clean mental model is: OAuth = authorization (access/permissions); OIDC = authentication (identity) built on OAuth. “Sign in with Google” is OIDC; “let this app post to your timeline” is OAuth. OIDC uses short token TTLs plus refresh tokens for continuous authentication — the right answer for microservices spanning multiple cloud environments.

The JWT (JSON Web Token) deserves its own paragraph because it is the carrier for OIDC identity and appears in many modern authorization schemes. A JWT has exactly three parts separated by dots: the Header (specifies the token type and the signing algorithm, e.g. HMAC SHA256 or RSA SHA256), the Payload (contains the claims — a JSON object with assertions about the user such as sub, email, role), and the Signature (a cryptographic value that verifies the token has not been tampered with). JWTs are compact, self-contained, and digitally signed — the server can verify them without a database lookup, which makes them ideal for stateless API authentication. Know the three-part structure: Header.Payload.Signature.

Before OIDC, there was OpenID (Legacy) — an earlier authentication-only standard for user-centric SSO without password sharing. It is largely superseded by OIDC today. Its primary risk was credential phishing through federated login redirects to a fake IdP — a risk OIDC inherits in a different form. The primary risk of OIDC that the exam flags is transitive trust: because every federated application trusts the IdP, a single IdP compromise exposes every application that relies on it. This is the “all eggs in one basket” problem of federation at scale. Related: when SAML session tokens are not revoked when a user account is disabled, the organisation loses the ability to enforce real-time deprovisioning — the session token remains valid even after the HR system marks the user as terminated, leaving a window where an ex-employee or attacker using a stolen token can still access federated services.

Two more federation terms complete the picture. WS-Federation is a Microsoft-led standard (part of the WS-* family) that provides federation primarily in Microsoft-centric environments — functionally an alternative to SAML for similar web-SSO problems. Use the CISSP heuristics the exam rewards: API access or delegated resource access → OAuth 2.0. Identity verification and SSO → OIDC. Legacy enterprise browser-based SSO → SAML. Continuous multi-cloud microservice authentication → OIDC with short TTL + refresh tokens. The technology split: SAML is XML-based and enterprise-browser-centric; OAuth/OIDC are JSON/JWT-based and suited to modern mobile apps, single-page apps, and REST APIs.

🌟 Exam Insight

Cannot-miss distinctions: IdP asserts, SP/RP relies. SAML = XML, enterprise SSO. OAuth 2.0 = AUTHORIZATION only (access tokens), NOT identity. OIDC = AUTHENTICATION on top of OAuth (JWT ID token). JWT = Header.Payload.Signature. OIDC primary risk = transitive trust (IdP breach → all federated apps exposed). SAML session tokens not revoked on disable = real-time deprovisioning failure. Heuristics: API → OAuth; SSO/identity → OIDC; legacy enterprise browser → SAML.

5.3 — Identity as a Service

Section 13 — IDaaS: Cloud Identity, On-Premise vs Cloud IAM and Integration

IDaaS

Extension of federated identity delivered as SaaS — SSO + federated IdM in the cloud.

Top Risk

Availability is CRITICAL — IDaaS outage = enterprise-wide authentication failure.

Loss of Control

Outsourcing identity management to a third party means trusting their uptime and breach defences.

Latency Risk

Termination changes may not propagate immediately — revoked access may lag in cloud.

Identity as a Service (IDaaS) is an extension of federated identity delivered as a Software-as-a-Service offering — think Okta, Azure Active Directory, or Ping Identity hosted in the cloud. IDaaS enables access management across teams, business units, and partner organisations, and is the standard approach for connecting SaaS applications to a central identity authority. Its value proposition is rapid setup, built-in federation connectors, and lower capital cost compared to building and running your own identity infrastructure. When the exam asks what is essential for federated authentication in a cloud-heavy environment, IDaaS is the answer.

There are three deployment models and the exam wants you to distinguish them. On-premise IAM gives the organisation total control over hardware, software, and security settings, but comes with high capital cost and a full maintenance burden — the organisation is responsible for uptime, patching, and breach response. It may need additional plugins or gateways to federate with cloud services. Cloud-native IDaaS offers rapid setup, lower CapEx, and built-in federation connectors for popular SaaS apps, but the organisation surrenders direct control and must trust the provider’s uptime and security posture. Multi-tenant logs are often opaque — you cannot see activity from other tenants — which creates auditing gaps. Hybrid IAM (synchronising on-premise Active Directory with cloud IDaaS) gives the smoothest migration path and provides a single identity usable everywhere, but incurs dual-maintenance overhead and remains exposed to third-party provider risks.

The two key concerns with IDaaS the exam returns to repeatedly are: (1) Availability is critical — if the IDaaS provider goes down, every user across every connected application cannot authenticate, making IDaaS itself a high-impact single point of failure; and (2) loss of control — the organisation outsources the most sensitive function in its security architecture, identity management, to an external party it cannot fully audit or direct. Cost is a significant implementation factor but it is a secondary concern compared to these two.

When deploying cloud IAM, the exam expects you to know six specific integration considerations. APIs: cloud IAM may need custom connectors or gateway plugins to communicate with legacy on-premise applications. Authorization mapping: access control rules written for an on-premise environment may need to be rewritten to match the cloud provider’s attribute model. Audit: multi-tenant cloud environments limit full log access — you can see your own tenant’s activity but not the infrastructure beneath it. Privacy: user attributes (department, role, location) now travel outside the corporate network to a third-party provider, raising data-protection obligations. Latency: when a user is terminated and access is revoked, the change may not propagate immediately to all cloud services, leaving a window where the former employee’s cloud session tokens remain valid — this is the latency risk and is a specific exam scenario. App identity: middleware and automated processes need their own service identities that the cloud IAM system must manage alongside human users. On-premise IAM components must connect to cloud mechanisms via encrypted communication paths to protect credential and identity data in transit.

🌟 Exam Insight

IDaaS top two concerns: Availability (IdP outage = no one authenticates anywhere) and Loss of Control (identity management outsourced to third party). Latency risk: termination changes may not propagate immediately — revoked user may still authenticate via a live session token. When a scenario says an organisation has no in-house identity staff and uses Active Directory, the best option is an on-premises third-party identity service (not full cloud IDaaS). On-premise ↔ cloud connections must use encrypted communication paths.

5.5 — Provisioning Lifecycle

Section 14 — The Identity Lifecycle: Joiners, Movers, Leavers and Access Reviews

JML

Joiner · Mover · Leaver — provision, adjust, deprovision.

Privilege Creep

Access accumulates on “movers” — the top reason for access reviews.

Access Review

Periodic recertification — confirm each access is still warranted.

Deprovisioning

Disable on exit — fastest path to revoke; orphan accounts are a breach risk.

An identity is not a one-time grant; it has a lifecycle that the organisation must manage from the day someone joins to the day they leave. The full IAM lifecycle has five phases the exam may name: Provisioning (account creation, identity proofing, role assignment, credential issuance) → Access Granted (authentication, authorisation, least-privilege enforcement) → Access In Use (monitoring, audit logging, User/Entity Behaviour Analytics — UBA/UEBA) → Periodic Review (access recertification, entitlement audit, orphan account check) → Deprovisioning (account disabled, credentials revoked, badge retrieved, NDA reviewed). This lifecycle is how Zero Trust is operationalised: trust is continuously verified at every stage, not assumed once at provisioning.

The industry shorthand for the human side is JML — Joiner, Mover, Leaver. Provisioning happens for a joiner: a new employee’s accounts and access are created, ideally driven automatically from the authoritative HR system and granted strictly according to least privilege for their role. The mover is the employee who transfers, gets promoted, or changes departments, and this is where the most insidious problem in identity management lives: privilege creep (also called access creep or entitlement creep). Each time someone moves, they tend to gain the new role’s access but rarely lose the old role’s access, so over years an employee silently accumulates a sprawling, excessive set of permissions that violates least privilege.

The control that catches privilege creep is the account access review — a periodic recertification in which managers and data owners re-examine who has access to what and formally confirm that each grant is still justified, revoking anything that is not. This is one of the most heavily tested operational concepts in Domain 5: when a question describes an employee who has changed roles several times and now holds far more access than their current job needs, the answer is almost always “conduct (or conduct more frequent) access reviews / recertification”. Reviews should be risk-based: privileged and high-sensitivity accounts get reviewed more often than ordinary users. Periodic recertification is also the primary control that prevents orphan account accumulation.

Before deprovisioning, the exam also tests privilege escalation — and distinguishes two directions. Vertical privilege escalation is what most people picture: a regular user elevates to a higher-privilege level, such as a standard employee exploiting a vulnerability to gain administrator rights. Horizontal privilege escalation is the subtler and often overlooked cousin: a user gains access to another user’s resources at the same privilege level, without elevating their rights — think of a bank customer accessing a different customer’s account. Horizontal privilege escalation across a network is also called lateral movement. Both types are attacks, but they exploit different weaknesses: vertical attacks exploit missing boundaries between privilege tiers; horizontal attacks exploit missing boundaries between accounts of equal rank.

Deprovisioning handles the leaver, and the exam is precise about the sequence. When an employee is terminated the first action is always to disable the account — even before the termination conversation if a hostile termination is anticipated, to prevent retaliation. After the account is disabled: recover physical property (badge, laptop, access cards), conduct the exit interview, and review the NDA obligations. The standard secure practice is to disable rather than immediately delete the account (disabling instantly cuts access while preserving the audit trail and any encryption keys; deletion can destroy evidence and leave encrypted data unrecoverable). Accounts that survive a departure become orphaned accounts — still-valid credentials belonging to no current employee — a prime target for attackers and former staff.

The lifecycle also covers special account types and privileged operations. Service accounts — non-human identities used by applications and automated processes — are dangerous precisely because they are often forgotten: they tend to have high privilege, passwords that never change, and no owner watching them, so they must be inventoried, tightly scoped, and their secrets rotated and vaulted. Privilege escalation through tools like sudo must be controlled and audited so that elevated actions are traceable to an individual. This whole area is the domain of Privileged Access Management (PAM), which combines credential vaulting, session recording, just-in-time elevation, and tight review of every privileged identity.

🌟 Exam Insight

Lifecycle phases: Provisioning → Access Granted → Access In Use → Periodic Review → Deprovisioning. Excessive access from role changes = privilege creep; cure = access recertification. Termination sequence: disable account FIRST (even before the conversation for hostile terminations) → recover property → exit interview → review NDA. Un-deprovisioned accounts = orphan accounts. Vertical escalation = higher privilege tier; horizontal escalation = lateral movement at the same privilege level. Always disable, not delete at termination.

5.6 — AAA Protocols & Attacks

Section 15 — RADIUS, TACACS+, Diameter, and Access-Control Attacks

RADIUS

UDP. Encrypts only the password. Combines authN+authZ. Common for Wi-Fi/VPN.

TACACS+

TCP. Encrypts the whole payload. Separates AAA. Cisco, device admin.

Diameter

RADIUS successor — more reliable, used in mobile/4G networks.

Session Hijacking

Steal a live session token/cookie — counter with timeouts & TLS.

Sub-domain 5.6 operationalises everything from the previous sections into the actual protocols enterprises deploy. The central comparison is the AAA protocols — so named because they provide Authentication, Authorization, and Accounting for network access (think Wi-Fi, VPN, and switch/router administration). RADIUS (Remote Authentication Dial-In User Service) is the workhorse for remote and wireless access. Its exam-critical properties: it runs over UDP, it combines authentication and authorization into a single step, and — crucially — it encrypts only the password in the access request, leaving the rest of the packet (including the username and accounting data) in the clear. RADIUS is an open standard, so it is vendor-neutral and ubiquitous.

TACACS+ (Terminal Access Controller Access-Control System Plus) is the Cisco-developed alternative, and the exam wants you to contrast it point-for-point with RADIUS. TACACS+ runs over TCP (more reliable, connection-oriented), it encrypts the entire payload rather than just the password (more confidential), and it separates the three A’s into independent functions — which is exactly why it is preferred for device administration: you can grant an operator the right to authenticate to a router and run a narrow set of commands (fine-grained authorization) while logging each command separately (granular accounting). The memory hook: RADIUS = UDP, password-only encryption, combined AA, network access; TACACS+ = TCP, full encryption, separated AAA, device administration. Diameter is the modern successor to RADIUS — more reliable and feature-rich, with broader attribute support — and shows up heavily in mobile/cellular (LTE/4G) networks; remember it as “RADIUS, upgraded.”

Finally, the domain closes the loop with the attacks against access control, because knowing how a control fails is how the exam confirms you understand it. The headline is session hijacking: once you have authenticated, the server tracks you with a session token (often a cookie), and if an attacker can steal or guess that token — by sniffing unencrypted traffic, cross-site scripting, or predicting a weak token — they can impersonate your live session without ever knowing your password. The countermeasures tie straight back to Section 8: transmit tokens only over TLS so they cannot be sniffed, use long random unpredictable session IDs, regenerate the ID after login, and enforce short idle and absolute timeouts so a stolen token expires quickly. Related attacks worth naming are session fixation (the attacker plants a known session ID on the victim before login — defeated by regenerating the ID at authentication) and replay attacks (capturing and re-sending valid authentication data — defeated by the time-stamps, nonces, and one-time values we saw in OTP and Kerberos). And tying back to accountability from Section 1: robust logging and monitoring of authentication and authorization events is what lets you detect these attacks in the first place — the “auditing” that makes accountability real.

🌟 Exam Insight

The RADIUS vs TACACS+ table is near-guaranteed: RADIUS — UDP, encrypts only the password, combines authN+authZ, vendor-neutral, used for network/remote access. TACACS+ — TCP, encrypts the whole payload, separates all three A’s, Cisco, ideal for device administration. Diameter succeeds RADIUS (mobile/4G). For session hijacking, the defences are TLS + strong random session IDs + short timeouts + regenerate ID after login.

✓

Domain 5 — Wrap-Up

Bringing It All Together

Step back and see the arc of the whole domain as a single story about one identity. It is proofed and registered (Section 3), it identifies and then authenticates with one or more of the three factors (Sections 4–8), it is authorized by a model and enforced by a mechanism (Sections 9–10), it reaches many systems through SSO, Kerberos, and federation (Sections 11–12), it is extended across cloud boundaries via IDaaS (Section 13), it is governed across its lifecycle from provisioning to deprovisioning — with privilege escalation guarded at every turn (Section 14), and every step is carried by real protocols (RADIUS, TACACS+, Diameter) and watched for attacks (Section 15). That is IAM end to end — the gatekeeping logic of the entire security profession.

If you are revising under time pressure, prioritise the highest-yield discriminators: the IAAA order and what each step means; multi-factor means different factor types not multiple credentials; Type I (FRR) vs Type II (FAR) errors and the lower-CER rule; user acceptance as the top biometric challenge; DAC/MAC/RBAC/ABAC trigger words with RBAC-is-a-form-of-DAC and ABAC-uses-XACML; Kerberos Golden (TGT, domain) vs Silver (ST, service) tickets plus synchronised clocks; OAuth-is-authorization vs OIDC-is-authentication vs SAML-is-XML; OIDC transitive trust risk; IDaaS availability and latency risks; three JIT types including ephemeral accounts; privilege creep cured by access reviews; disable-first on termination; horizontal (lateral) vs vertical escalation; and RADIUS-versus-TACACS+ side by side. Master those and you have the spine of Domain 5 — everything else hangs naturally off it.

🌟 Final Exam Strategy

When a Domain 5 question feels ambiguous, label the subject and object, decide which IAAA stage it concerns, then pick the answer that best upholds least privilege and individual accountability. Those two principles are the tie-breakers behind the majority of correct IAM answers. For protocol selection: API → OAuth; SSO → OIDC; legacy enterprise browser → SAML; device admin → TACACS+; network/VPN access → RADIUS.

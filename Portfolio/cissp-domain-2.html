Domain 2 · 10% average exam weight

Asset Security

Protecting information and assets across their entire lifecycle — knowing what you have, classifying it by value, assigning ownership, handling and protecting it according to its classification, and finally retaining, sanitising, and destroying it defensibly.

Sub-domains

Exam outline areas.

Domain 2 follows the life of data — from identifying and classifying it, through establishing handling rules, provisioning it securely and managing its lifecycle, to ensuring appropriate retention and selecting the right data-security controls.

2.1 — Identify & classify information and assets

Determining what you have and how sensitive it is — data classification and asset classification based on the value of the asset to the organisation.

2.2 — Establish handling requirements

Defining the rules for how information and assets are marked, stored, transmitted, and destroyed at each classification level — the handling standards that flow from the label.

2.3 — Provision information & assets securely

Establishing ownership, maintaining an accurate asset inventory, and practising disciplined asset management so nothing is unaccounted for.

2.4 — Manage data lifecycle

The full lifecycle — data roles (owner, controller, custodian, processor, user, subject), collection, location, maintenance, retention, remanence, and destruction.

2.5 — Ensure appropriate asset retention

Keeping assets only as long as needed and planning for End of Life (EOL) and End of Support (EOS) — unsupported assets become unpatched liabilities.

2.6 — Determine data security controls & compliance

Protecting the three data states (at rest, in transit, in use), scoping & tailoring, standards selection, and protection methods including DRM, DLP, and CASB.

Sub-domain 2.1

Identify & classify information and assets.

The starting point of the whole domain: knowing what you hold and grading it. The exam expects you to separate classification (by sensitivity/value) from categorization (by impact), to know both the government and commercial schemes, and to recite the classification process in order.

2.1

Information & Asset Classification — the Value Principle

DriverValue / sensitivity of the asset
GoalProportional, cost-effective protection
Who assignsThe data / asset owner (business role)
Two failuresOver-classify = waste; under-classify = risk

Classification is the process of grouping information and assets into tiers based on the harm that would result if their confidentiality, integrity or availability were lost, and then attaching a label to each tier. The entire reason it exists is economics of security: you have a finite budget, so you spend the most protecting what matters most. You cannot defend everything equally, and you shouldn't try.

Asset classification extends the same idea beyond data to the things that hold and process it — servers, laptops, mobile devices, network gear, applications, and even facilities. Assets are tangible (hardware, media, paper) or intangible (data, intellectual property, software, reputation, goodwill). An asset typically inherits the classification of the most sensitive data it stores or handles: a laptop that holds 'Confidential' data is itself treated as a 'Confidential' asset.

Benefits the exam wants you to recognise: classification demonstrates due care, supports regulatory compliance, focuses budget, and tells everyone — from the help desk to the CISO — exactly how each piece of data must be handled, stored, transmitted and destroyed.

Exam tip: Classification is always based on value/sensitivity, and it is assigned by the owner, never by IT or the custodian. If an answer choice has IT picking the classification, it's wrong.
2.1

Classification Schemes — Government vs. Commercial

Gov / MilitaryTop Secret · Secret · Confidential · (Unclassified)
Harm testGrave / Serious / Damage to national security
CommercialConfidential · Private · Sensitive · Public
RuleNumber of levels is a business decision

The government/military scheme is defined by the harm to national security if disclosed: Top Secret = exceptionally grave damage; Secret = serious damage; Confidential = damage. Below those sit Sensitive but Unclassified (SBU / Controlled Unclassified Information, CUI) and Unclassified. Memorise the order and the harm words — the exam pairs them.

The commercial/private-sector scheme has no legal mandate, so labels vary, but a common four-tier model is Confidential (highest — trade secrets, would cause serious harm), Private (personal/PII — internal-only personal data), Sensitive (needs more protection than public, e.g. internal financials), and Public (no harm if disclosed). Some books swap 'Private' and 'Sensitive' order; the exam tests the concept (relative harm), not a single canonical word list.

Exam tip: Pair the harm word to the level — Top Secret ↔ grave, Secret ↔ serious, Confidential ↔ damage. And remember: the number of classification levels is chosen by the organisation; too many becomes unusable.
2.1

Classification vs. Categorization — the Trap Question

ClassificationBy sensitivity / value of the data
CategorizationBy impact to CIA (FIPS 199)
FIPS 199 levelsLow · Moderate · High
GuidanceFIPS 199 + NIST SP 800-60

ISC² deliberately blurs these two words, so lock down the difference now. Classification looks at the data itself and asks “how sensitive / valuable is this?” Categorization looks at an information system and asks “what would be the impact on confidentiality, integrity and availability if this system were compromised?”

Categorization is a U.S. federal (RMF) concept from FIPS 199, which defines three impact levels — Low, Moderate, High — applied separately to each leg of the CIA triad. NIST SP 800-60 helps map information types to those impact levels. The system's overall category uses the high-water mark — the highest impact rating across C, I and A.

Exam tip: See the words impact, FIPS 199, Low/Moderate/High, or high-water mark → categorization. See sensitivity, Top Secret, labels → classification.
2.1

The Classification Process — in Order

The exam likes “what comes first / next” questions. The defensible order is: (1) identify and inventory the assets; (2) define the classification levels and the criteria for each (in policy); (3) the owner determines and assigns each asset's classification; (4) apply the required labels/markings and select controls for that level; (5) document handling, storage, transmission and destruction requirements; and (6) periodically review and re-classify (or declassify) as value changes.

Sub-domain 2.2

Establish information & asset handling requirements.

Once a label exists, it must translate into concrete rules for how the asset is marked, handled, stored, moved and destroyed at every point in its life. This is where classification earns its keep.

2.2

Marking, Handling, Storage & Transmission Requirements

MarkingApply the classification to the asset
HandlingWho can access & how it's used
StorageEncryption + physical/logical protection
TransmissionEncrypt in transit; controlled channels

Marking & labeling physically or logically tag the asset with its classification so both humans (markings) and systems (labels) know how to treat it. Every copy, backup and derivative inherits the marking.

Handling covers the rules of use — need-to-know access, whether the data may be printed, copied, emailed externally, or taken off-site. Storage dictates protection at rest: encryption strength, locked rooms or safes for physical media, and access control. Transmission dictates protection in transit: approved encrypted channels (TLS, VPN, SFTP) and approved couriers for physical media. Higher classification tightens every one of these.

Two special handling concepts: collection limitation (only gather what you need) and declassification (formally lowering a classification when the data's sensitivity has genuinely decreased — it must be a deliberate, documented act, never an accident).

Exam tip: The weakest link in handling is usually people and process, not technology — e.g. an employee emailing a 'Confidential' file to a personal account. Handling rules + DLP + training address this.

Sub-domain 2.3

Provision information & assets securely.

You can only protect what you have inventoried and assigned to an owner. This sub-domain is about ownership, the asset inventory, and disciplined asset management.

2.3

Ownership — the Accountable Roles

Data / Asset OwnerAccountable; sets classification
System OwnerOwns the system that processes the data
Business / Mission OwnerOwns the process the system supports
Senior ManagementUltimately accountable for all of it

Ownership establishes accountability, and accountability is what makes every other control meaningful. The data owner (a.k.a. information owner) is a senior business person accountable for a data set — they classify it, define who may access it, and approve its protection requirements. The system owner is accountable for the security of the information system that stores or processes that data (patching, configuration, the system security plan). The business/mission owner owns the business process the system enables and ensures it delivers value within acceptable risk.

Above all of them, senior management holds ultimate accountability for the organisation's security — a recurring CISSP theme. Owners set direction and accept risk; custodians implement; users follow the rules.

Exam tip: “Accountable” almost always points to the owner (or senior management); “responsible for day-to-day protection” points to the custodian. Accountability cannot be delegated — responsibility can.
2.3

Asset Inventory & Asset Management

InventoryComplete register of all assets (CMDB)
TangibleHardware, media, facilities
IntangibleData, software, IP, licences, goodwill
Watch forShadow IT & unmanaged assets

An accurate asset inventory is the bedrock control of Domain 2 — it is literally the first two controls in the CIS Controls list (inventory of hardware, then software). Unknown assets cannot be patched, classified, or defended, and they are where breaches hide (shadow IT). The inventory is often maintained in a Configuration Management Database (CMDB) that records each asset, its owner, location, classification, and configuration baseline.

Asset management is the disciplined lifecycle around that inventory: hardware asset management (procurement → deployment → maintenance → secure disposal), software asset management (tracking installations and licences to avoid both legal exposure from over-deployment and waste from over-buying), and configuration management (keeping assets in a known, hardened, documented state). Each asset is tracked from cradle to grave so that nothing is lost, unpatched, or improperly disposed of.

Exam tip: If a question describes a breach that happened on a server “no one knew was still running,” the root-cause control gap is an incomplete asset inventory. Inventory + ownership is always step one.

Sub-domain 2.4

Manage the data lifecycle.

The heart of Domain 2. Data is born, lives, and must die securely. Here we cover the lifecycle stages, the full set of data roles, and every topic ISC² lists: collection, location, maintenance, retention, remanence and destruction.

2.4

The Data Lifecycle — Create to Destroy

Think of data moving through stages, each needing its own controls: Create / Collect (classify it the moment it is born; collect only what you need) → Store (encrypt at rest, back up, control access) → Use (protect it in memory; enforce least privilege) → Share (encrypt in transit; control who it goes to) → Archive (retain per policy in protected long-term storage) → Destroy (sanitise defensibly at end of retention). The classification assigned at the very first stage governs the controls applied at every later stage.

2.4

Data Roles in Full — Owner, Controller, Custodian, Processor, Steward, User, Subject

This is the single most tested topic in Domain 2. Learn each role's one-line essence and the exam's favourite contrasts.

Exam traps: (1) Owner vs Custodian — decides vs implements. (2) Controller vs Processor — the processor never decides purpose; it only follows instructions. (3) Custodian vs Steward — custodian protects the bits, steward curates the meaning.
2.4

Data Collection & Minimization

PrincipleCollect only what you need (minimization)
WhyData you don't hold can't be breached
Purpose limitationUse only for the stated purpose
DriverGDPR, privacy-by-design

The lifecycle begins at collection, and the golden rule is data minimization: gather only the data genuinely required for the defined purpose, and no more. Every extra field you collect is extra liability — more to secure, more to expose in a breach, more to disclose in litigation, and more regulatory obligation. Closely tied is purpose limitation (use data only for the purpose it was collected for) and consent where personal data is involved. These are core privacy-by-design and GDPR principles the exam now emphasises.

Exam tip: The best way to reduce the impact of a future data breach is often to not collect or to delete unnecessary data — minimization beats adding more controls to data you didn't need in the first place.
2.4

Data Location, Residency & Sovereignty

Data residencyWhere data is physically stored
Data sovereigntyData obeys the laws of where it sits
LocalizationLaw requiring data stay in-country
RiskCross-border transfer & cloud regions

In a cloud world, where your data physically lives is a security and legal issue. Data residency is the physical/geographic location of stored data. Data sovereignty is the principle that data is subject to the laws of the country in which it is located — so storing EU citizens' data on a server in another jurisdiction can subject it to that jurisdiction's surveillance and disclosure laws. Data localization laws go further and require certain data to remain within national borders.

The practical controls: keep at least one protected copy, know exactly which cloud region holds each dataset, and govern cross-border transfers with the right legal mechanisms (e.g. GDPR Standard Contractual Clauses). Maintaining multiple geographically separate copies also supports availability and disaster recovery.

Exam tip: Residency = where it sits; sovereignty = whose laws apply to it. A cloud question about “which country's laws govern our customer data” is a data-sovereignty question.
2.4

Data Maintenance

Data maintenance is keeping data accurate, consistent, available and protected throughout its useful life — integrity controls, version control, deduplication, keeping metadata and classifications current, and ensuring backups remain restorable. A practical exam angle is the online vs offline distinction: frequently used data stays on fast online storage with full protection; rarely used data can move to cheaper offline/near-line storage, but it still carries its classification and must remain protected and retrievable. Maintenance is also where you keep an asset's classification label in sync if its sensitivity changes.

2.4

Data Retention & Legal Hold

Retention policyHow long each data type is kept
Keep too longLiability & breach surface
Delete too soonCompliance / legal violation
Legal holdOverrides retention — do NOT delete

A data retention policy defines, for each data type, how long it must be kept, based on legal, regulatory, and business needs. It is a balancing act: keeping data too long increases breach exposure and e-discovery cost, while deleting it too soon can break compliance (e.g. tax records, healthcare records) or destroy evidence. Retention should be automated and consistent — selective or ad-hoc deletion looks like spoliation.

A legal hold (litigation hold) is a critical exception: when litigation is reasonably anticipated, the organisation must suspend normal destruction for the relevant data. Destroying data under legal hold — even on schedule — is spoliation of evidence and carries severe legal penalties. Legal hold always overrides the retention schedule.

Exam tip: If a question mentions pending or anticipated litigation, the right answer is legal hold — stop all destruction of relevant data, regardless of the retention policy.
2.4

Data Remanence — Why “Delete” Lies

RemanenceResidual data left after “deletion”
Delete / formatRemoves pointers, not the data
HDD riskMagnetic remanence recoverable in lab
SSD riskWear-leveling hides copies in spare cells

Data remanence is the residual representation of data that persists after you believe it has been erased. Deleting a file or doing a quick format only removes the file-system pointers — the actual blocks remain on the platter and are trivially recovered with free tools. This is the entire reason media sanitisation is a discipline.

The problem is worse on SSDs. Because of wear-leveling, the controller spreads writes across physical cells and remaps logical addresses, so a single logical overwrite may leave the original data sitting untouched in spare or over-provisioned cells that software can't even address. This is why traditional multi-pass overwriting is unreliable on SSDs and why the recommended methods are the drive's built-in ATA Secure Erase / NVMe Sanitize, cryptographic erase, or physical destruction.

Exam tip: Remanence is the concept; clearing/purging/destruction are the cures. A “format” never solves remanence on its own.
2.4

Data Destruction & Media Sanitization (NIST SP 800-88)

ClearOverwrite; resists software recovery; reuse OK
PurgeResists lab recovery (degauss / secure erase / crypto)
DestroyPhysical; media unusable afterwards
Decide byData sensitivity + will media be reused / leave?

NIST SP 800-88 is the authoritative media-sanitization standard, and its three levels — Clear, Purge, Destroy — are heavily tested. Clear overwrites user-addressable space to stop software/keyboard recovery; the media stays usable, fine for internal reuse. Purge applies stronger methods (degaussing, ATA Secure Erase / NVMe Sanitize, or cryptographic erase) that defeat even laboratory recovery; appropriate before media leaves your control. Destroy physically ruins the media (shred, disintegrate, incinerate, pulverise, melt) so it can never be reused — the only acceptable choice for the most sensitive data or unrepairable/non-rewritable media.

You pick the level from two questions: how sensitive is the data, and will the media be reused or leave the organisation? And you must match the method to the media type — the classic exam trap below.

Exam tips: (1) Order of assurance: Clear < Purge < Destroy. (2) Degauss is magnetic-only and also destroys an HDD's servo tracks (no reuse). (3) For SSD/cloud, use cryptographic erase. (4) Always obtain a certificate of destruction when a third party disposes of media.

Sub-domain 2.5

Ensure appropriate asset retention.

Beyond data retention lies asset retention — deciding how long to keep hardware and software, and what to do when a vendor stops supporting them.

2.5

EOL, EOS / EOSL & Asset Retention

EOLEnd of Life — vendor stops selling/producing
EOS / EOSLEnd of Support/Life — no more patches
The dangerUnpatchable = permanent vulnerability
ActionReplace, isolate, or formally accept risk

End of Life (EOL) is the point at which a vendor stops actively selling/marketing a product, though support may continue for a while. End of Support / End of Service Life (EOS / EOSL) is the critical security milestone: the vendor will no longer issue patches or security updates. From that day forward, every newly discovered vulnerability in the product is permanent.

An EOS asset is therefore a standing liability that must be retired and replaced, or — if it absolutely must stay (legacy industrial systems, etc.) — tightly isolated/segmented with compensating controls, and the residual risk formally documented and accepted by management. Track EOL/EOS dates in the asset inventory so renewals and replacements are planned, not surprises. The flip side is asset retention proper: don't keep hardware longer than its secure, supportable life just because it still powers on.

Exam tip: Running End-of-Support software (e.g. an unsupported OS) on a production network is a classic exam “what's the biggest risk” — the answer is it can no longer be patched, so vulnerabilities are unfixable. Segment or replace.

Sub-domain 2.6

Determine data security controls & compliance requirements.

Finally, how you actually choose and apply controls: protect the three data states, scope and tailor a baseline, select the right standards, and deploy the modern data-protection toolkit — DRM, DLP, CASB and de-identification.

2.6

Protecting the Three Data States

At restAES-256, FDE, DB/file encryption
In transitTLS, VPN/IPSec, SSH/SFTP
In useEnclaves, TEE, homomorphic (emerging)
GoalCover all three — gaps get exploited

A control set is only complete if it protects data in all three states. At rest: symmetric encryption (AES-256), full-disk encryption, database/column and file-level encryption, backed by access control. In transit: encrypt the channel with TLS for web, VPN/IPSec for site-to-site, and SSH/SFTP for administrative transfers. In use is the hard one — data must be decrypted to be processed, so defences rely on hardware secure enclaves / Trusted Execution Environments, strict memory protection, and the still-maturing homomorphic encryption (compute on ciphertext without decrypting). Underpinning all of this is sound key management — encryption is only as strong as the protection of its keys.

Exam tip: “In use” is consistently the answer to “which state is hardest to protect?” because the data must be in cleartext in memory to be processed.
2.6

Baselines, Scoping & Tailoring

BaselineStandard starting set of controls
ScopingREMOVE controls that don't apply
TailoringADJUST the remaining controls to fit
SupplementADD controls for unique risks

You don't invent controls from a blank page; you start from a baseline — a predefined minimum set (e.g. a NIST SP 800-53 control baseline for a given impact level, or a CIS Benchmark). Then you make it fit your environment in two steps the exam tests precisely. Scoping is removing baseline controls that simply don't apply (no wireless? drop the wireless controls). Tailoring is adjusting the remaining controls — refining parameters, applying compensating controls, and supplementing with extra controls for risks unique to you — so the final set matches your actual risk.

Exam tip: Scoping = remove (decide which controls are in/out of scope). Tailoring = modify/adjust (and supplement). They are not synonyms.
2.6

Standards Selection & Compliance Frameworks

ISO 27001/27002ISMS & control catalogue (global)
NIST SP 800-53U.S. federal control catalogue (RMF)
PCI-DSSCardholder data (contractual)
PrivacyGDPR, HIPAA, ISO 27701, 27018

Standards selection is choosing which frameworks and standards your controls must satisfy, driven by your industry, geography and contracts. ISO/IEC 27001 defines an Information Security Management System and 27002 the control set; NIST SP 800-53 is the U.S. federal control catalogue used in the Risk Management Framework; PCI-DSS is contractually mandated for anyone handling payment cards; and privacy obligations come from GDPR, HIPAA, with ISO 27701 (privacy management) and 27018 (cloud PII) layered on top. The right answer in an exam scenario is usually the standard that matches the data and the jurisdiction in the question.

2.6

Data Protection Method — DRM

DRMControls content AFTER it's distributed
EnforcesNo copy/print/forward; expiry
IRMDRM applied to enterprise documents
TravelsProtection stays with the file

Digital Rights Management (DRM) enforces usage rules that travel with the content even after it leaves your network — preventing copying, printing or forwarding, limiting the number of opens, watermarking, or expiring access after a date. Applied to corporate documents it's often called Information Rights Management (IRM). The key distinction from DLP: DRM controls what an authorised recipient can do with content they already have; DLP stops content from leaving in the first place.

Exam tip: “Prevent a contractor from forwarding or printing a document we sent them” → DRM/IRM. “Stop the document from being emailed outside” → DLP.
2.6

Data Protection Method — DLP

DLPDetect & block sensitive data exfiltration
Network DLPScans email, web, uploads (in transit)
Endpoint DLPUSB, clipboard, print (in use)
Storage DLPDiscovers sensitive data (at rest)

Data Loss Prevention (DLP) inspects content and context to detect and block sensitive data from leaving the organisation. It maps neatly onto the three data states: network/gateway DLP watches data in transit (email, web uploads, FTP); endpoint DLP watches data in use on the device (copying to USB, clipboard, printing, screenshots); and storage/discovery DLP scans data at rest in file shares and databases to find where sensitive data is hiding. DLP relies on knowing what is sensitive — which comes straight from your classification labels and detection rules (pattern matching, fingerprinting, exact data matching).

Exam tip: DLP is most effective when paired with good classification — it can only block what it can identify. A common exam pairing: classify data first, then DLP enforces.
2.6

Data Protection Method — CASB

CASBPolicy broker between users & cloud
VisibilityFinds shadow IT / unsanctioned SaaS
ComplianceEnforces policy in cloud apps
ModesAPI-based or inline (proxy)

A Cloud Access Security Broker (CASB) sits between your users and cloud services to enforce security policy — the control point you otherwise lose when data moves to SaaS. It's usually framed around four pillars: Visibility (discover which cloud apps are in use, including unsanctioned shadow IT), Compliance (ensure cloud use meets regulations), Data Security (extend DLP and encryption into cloud apps), and Threat Protection (detect malware and anomalous account behaviour). CASBs deploy either API-based (out-of-band, inspecting data already in the cloud) or inline/proxy (in the live traffic path, able to block in real time).

Exam tip: CASB is the answer when the scenario is about gaining control and visibility over cloud/SaaS usage — especially discovering and governing shadow IT.
2.6

De-identification — Anonymization, Pseudonymization, Tokenization & Masking

A favourite modern exam cluster: techniques that reduce the sensitivity of data so it can be used more freely. Anonymization irreversibly removes all identifiers so data can never be linked back to a person — done properly it falls outside privacy laws like GDPR (but beware re-identification through correlation). Pseudonymization replaces identifiers with a reversible token/alias, keeping a separate mapping — still personal data under GDPR because it can be reversed. Tokenization swaps a sensitive value for a non-sensitive token, with the real value held in a secure token vault (heavily used for PCI/credit cards). Data masking / obfuscation hides data with realistic-looking but fake values, ideal for test/dev and screens (e.g. showing only the last 4 digits of a card).

Exam tip: Only anonymization (true, irreversible) takes data outside GDPR. Pseudonymization is still personal data because it can be reversed. Tokenization is the PCI go-to for card numbers.
★

High-Yield Exam Cheat Sheet — Rapid-Fire Distinctions

Owner vs Custodian: owner is accountable and sets classification; custodian is responsible and implements protection.

Controller vs Processor: controller decides purpose & means; processor only acts on the controller's instructions.

Classification vs Categorization: sensitivity/value (labels) vs impact to CIA (FIPS 199 Low/Mod/High).

Label vs Marking: label = system-readable (enforces access); marking = human-readable (informs people).

Policy / Standard / Baseline / Procedure / Guideline: all mandatory except guidelines.

Clear < Purge < Destroy: overwrite (reuse) → lab-proof (degauss/secure-erase/crypto) → physical.

Degaussing: magnetic media only — useless on SSD, flash & optical; use crypto-erase or destroy.

Remanence: delete/format leaves recoverable data; sanitization is the cure.

Scoping vs Tailoring: remove non-applicable controls vs adjust/supplement the rest.

DRM vs DLP: control content after it's sent vs stop it from leaving. CASB = control cloud/shadow IT.

Legal hold overrides retention — never destroy data subject to it (spoliation).

Data sovereignty = data obeys the laws of where it physically resides.

✓

Domain 2 in One Breath

Inventory every asset and give each an owner. Classify by value (don't confuse it with impact-based categorization); carry the label via system-readable labels and human-readable markings; and write the handling, storage and transmission rules that flow from it. Manage the whole lifecycle — collect the minimum, mind location/sovereignty, maintain integrity, retain per policy (honour legal holds), and at end of life defeat remanence with the right sanitization (Clear → Purge → Destroy; crypto-erase for SSD/cloud). Retire EOL/EOS assets before they become unpatchable. Choose controls by protecting the three data states, scoping & tailoring a baseline, selecting the right standards, and layering DRM, DLP, CASB and de-identification. Then assess & review — forever.

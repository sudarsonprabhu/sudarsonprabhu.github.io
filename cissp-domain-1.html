Expert Instructor Guide

Domain 1 — the complete walkthrough, sub-domain by sub-domain.

Domain 1 is the heaviest-weighted domain (16%) and the conceptual foundation for the whole exam. This is your full lecture on it, marching through the official outline 1.1 to 1.12 and unpacking every topic the way ISC² tests it — precise definitions, the distinctions that trap people, and a concrete example for each. The three maps above are the skeleton; this is the muscle. Read it top to bottom and you will not need another source for Domain 1.

Sub-domain 1.1

Understand, adhere to & promote professional ethics.

Ethics is canon-one material — quite literally. Every CISSP is bound by the (ISC)² Code, and you must know its canons in order.

1.1

The (ISC)² Code of Professional Ethics

Canon 1Protect society, the common good & infrastructure
Canon 2Act honourably, honestly, justly, legally
Canon 3Diligent, competent service to principals
Canon 4Advance & protect the profession

The Code has a preamble and four canons that are applied in strict ORDER of priority — this ordering is the exam's favourite ethics question. When two canons conflict, the lower-numbered one wins: protecting society and the public (Canon 1) outranks serving your employer/client (Canon 3). Beyond the (ISC)² Code, organisations adopt their own codes of conduct, and the field has historical ethics references like the Internet Architecture Board's RFC 1087 ("Ethics and the Internet") and the Computer Ethics Institute's Ten Commandments.

Exam tip: If a scenario pits your duty to your employer against the public good, choose the public good — Canon 1 always outranks Canon 3.

Sub-domain 1.2

Understand & apply security concepts.

The bedrock vocabulary of the whole CISSP — the CIA triad and the supporting concepts.

1.2

CIA Triad, DAD & the Supporting Concepts

ConfidentialityPrevent unauthorised disclosure
IntegrityPrevent unauthorised alteration
AvailabilityEnsure timely, reliable access
DAD (opposite)Disclosure, Alteration, Destruction

The CIA triad is the goal of all security. Confidentiality keeps data secret (encryption, access control); Integrity keeps it accurate and unaltered (hashing, checksums, change control); Availability keeps it accessible when needed (redundancy, backups, DDoS protection). Their opposites form DAD (Disclosure, Alteration, Destruction). Supporting concepts the exam adds: authenticity (genuineness), non-repudiation (can't deny an action — via digital signatures), plus privacy and safety. Underpinning access is IAAA: Identification → Authentication → Authorization → Accountability (auditing).

Sub-domain 1.3

Evaluate & apply security governance principles.

Aligning security to the business, fixing accountability, and proving leadership did the right thing.

1.3

Governance, Alignment & Due Care / Due Diligence

AlignmentSecurity enables business strategy
Accountable vs responsibleAnswerable vs assigned-to-do
Due diligenceKNOWING what to do (investigate)
Due careDOING it (prudent action)

Security governance is senior management providing strategic direction so security aligns to and enables business goals — it nests inside corporate governance. Roles must be clear: the accountable party is ultimately answerable (can't delegate), the responsible party does the work. Leadership demonstrates due diligence (the ongoing research to KNOW what should be done) and due care (actually DOING what a prudent person would — implementing the controls). Failing due care is negligence and creates legal liability. Governance is supported by frameworks such as COBIT (IT governance), ITIL (service management), and ISO/IEC 27001 (ISMS).

Sub-domain 1.4

Legal, regulatory & compliance issues.

A holistic view of the law that touches information security — crime, intellectual property, privacy, and cross-border controls.

1.4

Types of Law & Computer Crime

CriminalAgainst society; jail; beyond reasonable doubt
Civil (tort)Between parties; money; preponderance
AdministrativeRegulatory agency rules & fines
Legal systemsCommon, civil (code), religious, customary

Three categories of law matter. Criminal law punishes acts against society (imprisonment/fines; proof beyond a reasonable doubt). Civil/tort law resolves disputes between parties (monetary damages; proof by preponderance of the evidence). Administrative/regulatory law is agency-made rules (HIPAA, PCI enforcement). The world also runs different legal systems: common law (precedent, US/UK), civil/code law (statutes, most of Europe), plus religious and customary law. Computer-crime statutes (e.g. the U.S. Computer Fraud and Abuse Act) criminalise unauthorised access and damage.

1.4

Intellectual Property, Privacy & Cross-Border Controls

IP typesTrade secret, patent, copyright, trademark
PrivacyGDPR, HIPAA, GLBA, CCPA; OECD principles
Import/exportITAR, EAR, Wassenaar (crypto/dual-use)
TransborderData sovereignty; lawful transfer needed

Intellectual property (covered in detail on the Layout 2 map): trade secret (forever if kept secret), patent (~20 yrs, public disclosure), copyright (expression, automatic, life+70), trademark (brand, renewable). Privacy law — GDPR (EU, 72-hour breach reporting, up to 4% global turnover fines), HIPAA (health), GLBA (finance), CCPA (California) — all build on the OECD fair-information principles. Cross-border movement of encryption and dual-use technology is restricted by ITAR, EAR and the Wassenaar Arrangement, and transborder data flows trigger data-sovereignty rules requiring approved transfer mechanisms. Remember: you cannot achieve privacy without security.

Sub-domain 1.5

Requirements for investigation types.

Different investigations carry different burdens of proof and evidence rules.

1.5

Administrative, Criminal, Civil, Regulatory & Industry-Standard

AdministrativeInternal policy; lowest formality
CriminalBeyond a reasonable doubt
CivilPreponderance of the evidence
Regulatory / industryAgency rules / standards (e.g. PCI)

The investigation type sets the rules. Administrative (internal) investigations have the lowest bar and handle policy violations. Criminal investigations require the highest standard — beyond a reasonable doubt — and the strictest evidence handling (because liberty is at stake). Civil uses the lower preponderance of the evidence. Regulatory investigations enforce government rules, and industry-standard investigations check against frameworks like PCI-DSS. Because a matter can escalate to criminal, always preserve evidence and chain of custody to the highest standard from the outset (this ties directly to Domain 7).

Sub-domain 1.6

Security policy, standards, procedures & guidelines.

The document hierarchy — and the one type that's optional.

1.6

The Policy Document Hierarchy

PolicyMandatory, high-level intent (the "why")
Standards / BaselinesMandatory specifics & minimums
ProceduresMandatory step-by-step (the "how")
GuidelinesRECOMMENDED — the only optional one

Governance documents form a hierarchy. Policy is the top-level, mandatory statement of intent from senior management. Standards mandate specific technologies and configurations; baselines set the mandatory minimum security level; procedures give mandatory step-by-step instructions. Guidelines are the ONLY discretionary, recommended document — everything else is mandatory. The exam loves to ask which document is optional (guidelines) and which is highest-level (policy).

Sub-domain 1.7

Business Continuity requirements & the BIA.

Domain 1 owns the planning side of continuity; the BIA is its engine.

1.7

BIA, Scope & Time Objectives

BIAFind critical functions, set MTD/RTO/RPO
MTDMax tolerable downtime (= RTO + WRT)
RPOMax data loss → backup frequency
ApprovalSenior management owns & signs off

Domain 1 covers identifying, analysing, assessing, prioritising and implementing BC requirements — the foundation that Domain 7 later executes. The Business Impact Analysis (BIA) is the cornerstone: it identifies critical business functions, the resources they depend on, and the maximum tolerable downtime (MTD) for each, then derives the RTO (time to recover) and RPO (acceptable data loss). It quantifies impact (quantitative + qualitative) and must be approved by senior management, who own the continuity strategy. The BCP is the broad plan; the DRP is its IT-recovery subset.

Exam tip: The BIA — not the plan — is the step that identifies critical functions and sets the MTD. And MTD = RTO + WRT.

Sub-domain 1.8

Personnel security policies & procedures.

People are the biggest risk — secure the whole employment lifecycle.

1.8

Hire → Employ → Terminate, Safely

HiringScreening, background checks, NDA
EmploymentAUP, least privilege, SoD, job rotation
Mandatory vacationDetects hidden fraud
TerminationRevoke access immediately, exit interview

Personnel security spans the whole lifecycle. Hiring: candidate screening, background and reference checks, and signing an NDA and acceptable-use agreement. Employment: enforce least privilege and need-to-know, separation of duties (no one controls a whole critical process — prevents fraud), job rotation and mandatory vacation (which detect fraud), and ongoing awareness. Termination: a planned process — revoke all access immediately (ideally before/at notification), recover assets, disable accounts, and conduct an exit interview. Third-party/contractor personnel need the same controls.

Exam tip: SoD prevents fraud (needs collusion to beat); job rotation and mandatory vacation detect it. On termination, the priority is immediate access revocation.

Sub-domain 1.9

Understand & apply risk management concepts.

The heart of Domain 1 — identify, assess, and treat risk, with the math the exam loves.

1.9

Risk Concepts & the Quantitative Math

RiskThreat × Vulnerability (likelihood × impact)
SLEAsset Value × EF (exposure factor)
ALESLE × ARO (annual loss)
Residual riskWhat remains after controls

Risk exists where a threat can exploit a vulnerability, measured by likelihood × impact. Quantitative analysis uses the formula chain: SLE (Single Loss Expectancy) = Asset Value × EF (Exposure Factor); ARO = Annualised Rate of Occurrence; ALE = SLE × ARO. A control is cost-justified when it costs less than the ALE it removes. Qualitative analysis ranks risk on relative scales (heat maps). After treatment, residual risk remains — what's left after controls — and management must accept it within the organisation's risk appetite/tolerance.

1.9

Treatment, Controls & Frameworks

4 treatmentsAvoid, Transfer, Mitigate, Accept
By natureAdministrative, Technical, Physical
By functionDirective→Deterrent→Preventive→Detective→Corrective→Recovery→Compensating
FrameworksNIST RMF, ISO 31000, COSO, Risk IT

Four ways to treat risk: Avoid (stop the activity), Transfer (insurance/outsource — share it), Mitigate (reduce with controls), or Accept (retain within appetite). Mitigation applies controls, classified by nature (administrative / technical / physical) and by function (directive, deterrent, preventive, detective, corrective, recovery, compensating). A safeguard is a proactive control; a countermeasure is reactive to a specific threat. Anchor it all in a framework: NIST RMF (SP 800-37, 7 steps), ISO 31000 (generic risk), COSO (enterprise/financial internal control), and ISACA Risk IT.

Sub-domain 1.10

Threat modeling concepts & methodologies.

Find the threats by design, before attackers find them in production.

1.10

STRIDE, PASTA, DREAD & Friends

STRIDESpoof, Tamper, Repudiate, Info-disclose, DoS, Elevate
PASTA7-stage, risk-centric, attacker simulation
DREADRating: Damage, Reproduce, Exploit, Affected, Discover
OthersAttack trees, Trike, VAST

Threat modeling structures the hunt for threats early in design. STRIDE (Microsoft) classifies threats into six types — Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information disclosure, Denial of service, Elevation of privilege. PASTA (Process for Attack Simulation and Threat Analysis) is a seven-stage, risk-centric method that ties threats to business objectives and simulates real attacker behaviour. DREAD is a rating system (Damage, Reproducibility, Exploitability, Affected users, Discoverability) for ranking threats. Others worth recognising: attack trees, Trike, and VAST. Do it early — fixing design flaws on paper is far cheaper than in production.

Sub-domain 1.11

Supply Chain Risk Management (SCRM).

Your security is only as strong as your weakest supplier — and their suppliers.

1.11

Third-Party & Fourth-Party Risk

Third-partyYour direct vendors & suppliers
Fourth-partyYour vendors' vendors (sub-suppliers)
Bind itMSA, SLA, right-to-audit, min security reqs
VerifyAssessments, SOC 2, provenance checks

SCRM manages the risk introduced by suppliers across the hardware, software and service supply chain. Assess third parties (your direct vendors) AND fourth parties (their sub-suppliers, who you don't contract with but still depend on). Control them contractually with a Master Service Agreement (MSA), SLAs, minimum security requirements, breach-notification and right-to-audit clauses. Verify with on-site assessments, SOC 2 reports, and component provenance/integrity checks (to counter counterfeit or tampered hardware/software). Famous supply-chain attacks (SolarWinds) show why this is now its own outline area.

Sub-domain 1.12

Security awareness, education & training.

The human firewall — the most cost-effective control there is.

1.12

Awareness vs Training vs Education

AwarenessThe "WHAT" — recognition & attention
TrainingThe "HOW" — specific job skills
EducationThe "WHY" — deep understanding
ReinforcePhishing sims, gamification, metrics

The three escalating levels: Awareness changes behaviour and attention — the what (posters, phishing reminders, recognising threats). Training builds specific job skills — the how (secure-coding workshops, tool training). Education develops deeper, conceptual understanding — the why (degrees, certifications like the CISSP itself). Reinforce continuously with phishing simulations, gamification and metrics, and tailor content by role. Since most breaches start with a human action, this is among the highest-ROI controls you can deploy.

Accountable (answerable, can't delegate) vs responsible (does the work).

Criminal = beyond reasonable doubt; civil = preponderance; administrative = agency rules.

IP: trade secret (forever-if-secret), patent (~20y + disclosure), copyright (expression, automatic), trademark (renewable).

Policy hierarchy: policy → standards → baselines → procedures (all mandatory); guidelines = optional.

Awareness (what) / training (how) / education (why).

✓

Domain 1 in One Breath

Security exists to enable the business. Hold the (ISC)² ethics (canons in order, society first), pursue the CIA triad, and run security governance that aligns to corporate strategy with clear accountability and demonstrable due diligence & due care. Obey the law — criminal/civil/administrative, intellectual property, privacy (GDPR/OECD), and import/export — and match each investigation to its proof standard. Write a proper policy hierarchy (only guidelines optional), plan continuity from the BIA, and secure personnel across hire → employ → terminate (SoD prevents, rotation/vacation detect). At the core, do risk management: value assets, analyse risk (SLE → ALE, or qualitative), treat it (avoid / transfer / mitigate / accept) with the right controls, and accept the residual — all inside a framework (RMF / ISO 31000). Threat-model early (STRIDE/PASTA/DREAD), manage supply-chain risk (third & fourth parties), and build the human firewall with awareness, training & education.

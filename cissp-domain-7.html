Domain 7 — the complete walkthrough, sub-domain by sub-domain.

Domain 7 is the biggest, most operational domain on the exam (13%). This is your full lecture on it, marching through the official outline 7.1 to 7.15 and unpacking every topic the way ISC² tests it — precise definitions, the distinctions that trap people, and a concrete example for each. The six maps above are the skeleton; this is the muscle. Read it top to bottom and you will not need another source for Domain 7.

Sub-domain 7.1

Understand & comply with investigations.

When something goes wrong, you investigate — lawfully, methodically, and in a way that keeps evidence admissible. This area blends digital forensics with legal process.

7.1

Types of Investigation & Standards of Proof

CriminalBeyond a reasonable doubt (highest)
CivilPreponderance of evidence (>50%)
RegulatoryIndustry/agency rules & standards
AdministrativeInternal policy; lowest formality

The TYPE of investigation sets the burden of proof and how rigorously evidence must be handled. Criminal investigations (a law was broken) demand the highest standard — beyond a reasonable doubt — and can end in imprisonment, so evidence handling must be flawless. Civil cases (disputes between parties) use the lower preponderance of the evidence (more likely than not). Regulatory investigations are run by a government regulator against industry rules, and administrative ones are internal (policy violations) with the least formality.

The exam's favourite trap: matching the standard of proof to the investigation type. Memorise criminal = beyond reasonable doubt, civil = preponderance.

Exam tip: Because a criminal case may emerge later, always handle evidence to the highest (criminal) standard from the start — you can't retroactively fix a broken chain of custody.
7.1

Evidence — Types, the 5 Rules & Chain of Custody

Best evidence ruleProduce the original where possible
5 rulesAuthentic, Accurate, Complete, Convincing, Admissible
Chain of custodyWho/what/when/where, unbroken
AdmissibilityLawfully gathered + relevant

Evidence comes in types — real (the physical object/drive), direct (firsthand testimony), secondary (copies, admissible when the original is unavailable) — governed by the best-evidence rule (prefer the original). To be useful it must satisfy the five rules: authentic, accurate, complete (including exculpatory evidence), convincing, and admissible.

The single most important control is the chain of custody: a documented, unbroken record of everyone who handled each item, when, and why. Any gap lets the other side argue the evidence was altered and have it excluded. Integrity is proven with hashing — hash the evidence at acquisition and re-verify it later.

Exam tip: “Complete” means you must include evidence that helps the suspect too — cherry-picking destroys credibility and admissibility.
7.1

Digital Forensics — Order of Volatility & Sound Acquisition

Order of volatilityCapture most fleeting first (RAM → disk)
ImagingBit-for-bit copy; work on the copy
IntegrityHash original & image; must match
eDiscoveryProduce ESI for litigation (EDRM)

Digital forensics is the scientific collection and analysis of digital evidence. The golden rule is the order of volatility — collect the most fleeting data FIRST: CPU/cache → RAM (live/volatile evidence, lost at power-off) → disk (secondary storage) → archival/backups. Pulling the plug to “preserve” a machine destroys volatile memory evidence (running malware, encryption keys).

Acquire disks by making a bit-for-bit forensic image, then hash both the original and the image so you can prove they match; all analysis happens on the copy so the original is preserved. eDiscovery is the legal-process side — identifying, preserving and producing electronically stored information (ESI) under a litigation hold, following the EDRM model. Don't forget the locations the exam lists: computers, networks, mobile devices, and cloud/VM sources (which add custody and jurisdiction challenges).

Exam tip: Locard's Exchange Principle — “every contact leaves a trace” — is why forensic artifacts exist to be found. And MOM (Motive, Opportunity, Means) frames who could have done it.

Sub-domain 7.2

Conduct logging & monitoring activities.

You can't defend what you can't see. This area is about generating, centralising and analysing the telemetry that powers detection — IDS/IPS, SIEM, threat intelligence, and behaviour analytics.

7.2

IDS & IPS — Detection vs. Prevention, Signature vs. Anomaly

IDSDetects & alerts (out-of-band)
IPSDetects & blocks (inline)
SignatureKnown patterns; misses zero-day
Anomaly/behaviourBaseline deviation; catches unknown

An IDS detects and alerts on suspicious activity but sits out of band — it watches a copy of traffic and can't stop an attack itself. An IPS sits inline and can actively block. Both come as network (NIDS/NIPS) — watching a network segment — or host (HIDS/HIPS) — watching a single system's files, logs and processes.

Detection works two ways. Signature/knowledge-based matches known attack patterns — accurate for known threats, blind to zero-days. Anomaly/behaviour-based builds a baseline of “normal” and flags deviations — can catch unknown attacks but produces more false positives and needs a clean learning period.

Exam tip: A false negative (missed real attack) is more dangerous than a false positive. Anomaly detection lowers false negatives at the cost of more false positives.
7.2

SIEM, Log Management, Continuous & Egress Monitoring

SIEMAggregate + correlate logs, real-time alerts
Clipping levelThreshold before alerting (cut noise)
Continuous monitoringOngoing, near-real-time posture
Egress monitoringWatch data LEAVING (DLP, exfil)

A SIEM centralises logs from across the estate and correlates them in real time, turning thousands of individual events into a few high-fidelity alerts. It runs the whole log-management lifecycle: generation → transmission (securely) → collection/aggregation → normalization → analysis → retention → disposal. Use NTP-synchronised timestamps so events from different sources can be correlated, and protect logs from tampering (attackers love to wipe them).

Continuous monitoring means watching posture and controls ongoing rather than at a point in time (a NIST RMF idea). Egress (outbound) monitoring specifically watches data leaving the organisation to catch exfiltration — this is where DLP lives. Clipping levels suppress routine below-threshold events so analysts aren't drowned in noise.

7.2

Threat Intelligence, Threat Hunting & UEBA

Threat intelContext on adversaries (IOCs, TTPs)
Threat feedsStreams of indicators (IPs, hashes)
Threat huntingProactively seek hidden intruders
UEBABaseline users/entities, flag anomalies

Threat intelligence is contextual knowledge about adversaries — their indicators of compromise (IOCs) and tactics, techniques and procedures (TTPs, e.g. MITRE ATT&CK) — delivered via threat feeds. Threat hunting is the proactive assumption-of-breach discipline: actively searching the environment for attackers who slipped past automated defences, rather than waiting for an alert. UEBA (User and Entity Behavior Analytics) uses machine learning to baseline normal behaviour for users and devices and flag deviations — catching compromised accounts and insider threats that signatures miss.

Sub-domain 7.3

Perform Configuration Management.

Keeping every asset in a known, documented, hardened state — the antidote to configuration drift.

7.3

Provisioning, Baselining & Automation

ProvisioningStand up assets securely & consistently
BaseliningDefined secure standard config
Automation / IaCRepeatable, drift-free config as code
GoalPrevent configuration drift

Configuration management (CM) ensures systems are built and maintained to a known good state. Provisioning stands up new assets consistently and securely (not hand-built snowflakes). A baseline is the documented secure standard configuration — the reference every system must match (e.g. a hardened CIS Benchmark image). Automation — including Infrastructure as Code — applies and re-applies that baseline repeatably, eliminating configuration drift (the slow divergence from the standard that opens security gaps). CM is the partner of change management: change management approves a change, CM records the resulting state in the baseline/CMDB.

Sub-domain 7.4

Apply foundational security operations concepts.

The classic operational controls that limit what any one person can do — and the damage any one account can cause.

7.4

Need-to-Know, Least Privilege & Separation of Duties

Need-to-knowAccess only the DATA your job needs
Least privilegeOnly the PERMISSIONS your job needs
Separation of DutiesSplit critical tasks across people
Two-person controlTwo people to perform one action

Need-to-know and least privilege are siblings: need-to-know limits access to the specific information required, while least privilege limits the rights/permissions a user or process holds to the minimum required. Separation of Duties (SoD) divides a critical task so no single person can complete it alone — preventing fraud (the person who requests a payment can't also approve it). Related: two-person control / dual control (two people needed to act, e.g. launch keys), and collusion (the only way to beat SoD is for multiple people to conspire).

Exam tip: Need-to-know = data; least privilege = permissions. SoD prevents fraud; job rotation and mandatory vacation detect it.
7.4

Privileged Account Management, Job Rotation & SLAs

PAMVault, rotate, monitor admin accounts
Job rotationMove staff roles; detects fraud, builds cover
Mandatory vacationForced time off exposes hidden fraud
SLAContracted service/performance levels

Privileged Account Management (PAM) tightly controls powerful admin/root accounts — vaulting credentials, requiring check-out and just-in-time access, rotating passwords, and recording privileged sessions. These accounts are the keys to the kingdom, so they get extra monitoring. Job rotation moves people between roles, which both spreads knowledge (no single point of failure) and detects fraud when a successor finds irregularities. Mandatory vacation works the same way — forcing someone away from their duties surfaces fraud that depended on their daily concealment. SLAs formally define the service levels (uptime, response times) a provider must meet, with penalties for misses.

Sub-domain 7.5

Apply resource protection.

Protecting the media and data the organisation runs on, throughout its life.

7.5

Media Management & Protecting Data States

Media managementTrack, mark, store, sanitise media
At restEncrypt (AES), access control
In transitTLS / VPN / SFTP
End of lifeSanitise: clear / purge / destroy

Media management applies the full lifecycle to storage media: inventory and mark it with its classification, control where and how it's stored and transported (the most sensitive media gets the strongest handling), and sanitise it at end of life. This ties straight back to Domain 2 — protect data at rest (encryption, access control), data in transit (TLS/VPN), and defeat remanence when disposing of media (clearing → purging → destruction; crypto-erase or shred for SSDs). Source/library protection and proper labelling prevent the wrong media being mounted or leaked.

Sub-domain 7.6

Conduct incident management.

The structured lifecycle for handling adverse events — the order matters, and the exam tests it.

7.6

The Incident Management Lifecycle

Event vs incidentObservable vs adverse (harms CIA)
First (always)Preparation — before anything happens
OrderDetect → Respond → Mitigate → Report → Recover → Remediate → Learn
ContainmentMitigation = stop the spread first

An event is any observable occurrence; an incident is an event that harms or threatens confidentiality, integrity or availability. The ISC² lifecycle runs in order: Detection → Response → Mitigation → Reporting → Recovery → Remediation → Lessons Learned, all resting on advance Preparation. Containment (mitigation) comes before eradication and recovery — stop the bleeding first. Reporting must meet legal breach-notification deadlines (e.g. GDPR's 72 hours). Remediation fixes root cause so it can't recur, and lessons learned feeds back into preparation, closing the loop.

Sub-domain 7.7

Operate & maintain detection and preventative measures.

The defensive toolbox — from firewalls to honeypots — and how the generations of firewall differ.

7.7

Firewall Generations

Packet filterL3/4 headers; stateless; fast, dumb
StatefulTracks connection state
Proxy / AppL7; inspects content, breaks direct path
NGFW / WAFApp-aware + IPS / protects web apps

Firewalls evolved in generations. Packet-filtering (1st gen) checks IP/port headers statelessly — fast but easily fooled. Stateful inspection tracks the state of connections, so it knows a packet belongs to an established session. Proxy / application-layer firewalls operate at Layer 7, fully inspecting content and acting as an intermediary (no direct client-server path). Next-Generation Firewalls (NGFW) add application awareness, integrated IPS, and threat intelligence; a Web Application Firewall (WAF) specifically protects web apps from injection/XSS by inspecting HTTP.

7.7

Allow/Deny-listing, Sandboxing, Honeypots & AI Tools

Allow-listOnly approved runs (default deny)
SandboxingDetonate/run code in isolation
Honeypot / -netDecoy to lure & study attackers
AI/ML toolsBehavioural detection at scale

Allow-listing (permit only approved apps, deny all else) beats block-listing for stopping unknown/zero-day malware. Sandboxing runs suspicious code in an isolated environment to observe it safely — the basis of modern malware detonation. A honeypot is a decoy system designed to attract and study attackers; a honeynet is a whole decoy network of them — both yield threat intelligence and early warning (beware enticement vs unlawful entrapment). Third-party security services (e.g. MSSPs) and AI/ML-based tools add scale and behavioural detection beyond static signatures. Don't forget the malware fundamentals from the map: virus (needs host+user) vs worm (self-propagating) vs trojan (deception, no replication), and zero-day (no patch, signatures useless — behaviour-based defence only).

Sub-domains 7.8 & 7.9

Patch / vulnerability & change management.

Two tightly-coupled disciplines: find & fix weaknesses, and govern every production change.

7.8

Patch & Vulnerability Management

DiscoverThreat intel, vendor notices, scanning
Test firstNever patch straight to production
DeployAutomated (scale) or manual (sensitive)
Via change mgmtPatches are changes — control them

Patch management runs a loop: determine a patch is needed (threat intelligence, vendor notifications, and proactive scanning by agent, agentless or passive methods), test it in a non-production environment, then deploy it — automated for fleets, manual for sensitive systems — through change management with proper timing (emergency window for actively-exploited flaws, scheduled window otherwise). Vulnerability management is the broader cycle of identify → prioritise (CVSS) → remediate → verify, feeding the patch process.

7.9

Change Management & the Approval Boards

ProcessRequest → Assess → Approve → Test → Notify → Implement → Validate → Baseline
CCB / CABControl / Advisory board approval
ECABEmergency fast-track approval
AlwaysRollback plan + documentation

Change management ensures no modification hits production ad hoc. Every change is requested (RFC), impact-assessed, approved by the appropriate board, built and tested with a rollback plan, communicated, implemented, validated (functional + regression), and recorded in the baseline. CCB (Change Control Board) authorises and oversees; CAB (Change Advisory Board) advises on risk/impact; ECAB (Emergency CAB) fast-tracks urgent changes — documented retroactively. Classify changes as standard (routine, pre-approved) or emergency (urgent, expedited).

Exam tip: The purpose of change management is to prevent unintended outages and security gaps from uncontrolled changes — and to provide an audit trail. A rollback plan is mandatory.

Sub-domain 7.10

Implement recovery strategies.

Backups, RAID, high availability and recovery sites — the building blocks of resilience.

7.10

Backups & the Archive Bit

FullAll data; clears archive bit
IncrementalSince last backup; clears bit; slow restore
DifferentialSince last FULL; keeps bit; fast restore
3-2-1 rule3 copies, 2 media, 1 offsite

The exam loves the backup trio. Full copies everything and clears the archive bit. Incremental copies what changed since the last backup (full or incremental) and clears the bit — fastest to back up, slowest to restore (need full + every incremental in order). Differential copies what changed since the last full and does NOT clear the bit — backups grow daily but restore is fast (full + the latest differential). Validate backups with checksums and test restores, keep one copy offsite, and remember RPO drives backup frequency.

7.10

RAID, High Availability & Recovery Sites

RAID 0 / 1Stripe (speed) / Mirror (copy)
RAID 5 / 61-disk / 2-disk fault tolerance
HAClustering + redundancy, no SPOF
SitesCold → Warm → Hot → Mirror

RAID gives disk fault tolerance (NOT a backup!): RAID 0 stripes for speed with no redundancy, RAID 1 mirrors, RAID 5 survives one disk loss via parity, RAID 6 survives two. High availability removes single points of failure through clustering (nodes fail over) and redundancy (duplicate components). Recovery sites trade cost for speed: cold (space only, days–weeks), warm (some kit, hours–days), hot (ready, minutes–hours), mobile (portable), and mirror/redundant (real-time, instant). And spare parts readiness mirrors this: cold (shelf), warm (powered standby), hot (auto-failover). All recovery sites must be geographically remote.

Sub-domains 7.11 & 7.12

Disaster Recovery — processes & testing.

DR is the technical recovery of IT after a disaster, and you must test the plan to trust it.

7.11

DR Processes & the Time Objectives

RPOMax data loss (backward) → backup freq
RTOTime to restore the system (forward)
WRTTime to verify & resume operations
MTDTotal tolerable downtime = RTO + WRT

The DR process steps the exam lists: response, personnel, communications, assessment, restoration, training & awareness, and lessons learned. Underpinning DR are the time objectives. RPO is the most data you can lose (sets backup frequency). RTO is how long to get the system back. WRT is the extra time to validate and resume normal operations. MTD (Maximum Tolerable Downtime) is the total a function can be down before unacceptable harm — and crucially MTD = RTO + WRT. RTO must always be shorter than MTD.

7.12

Testing DR Plans — Low to High Risk

Read-throughChecklist review on paper
WalkthroughTabletop talk-through
Simulation / ParallelAct out / run alt site (prod up)
Full-interruptionShut prod down — riskiest

You climb the testing ladder as confidence grows: read-through/checklist (paper review) → walkthrough (tabletop discussion) → simulation (act out a scenario) → parallel (bring up the alternate site alongside production, which stays live) → full-interruption (actually shut production down and cut over — most thorough, riskiest, needs sign-off). Parallel proves the recovery site without risking production; full-interruption is the only test that proves everything but can cause real downtime if it fails.

Sub-domain 7.13

Business Continuity planning & exercises.

The big-picture programme that keeps the business alive — BCP is broader than DRP.

7.13

BCM, the BIA & BCP vs DRP

Goal #1Safety of people (always first)
BIAFind critical functions, set MTD/RTO/RPO
BCPKeep the whole business running
DRPRestore IT systems (subset of BCP)

Business Continuity Management focuses on the organisation's critical and essential functions. Its goals run in order: safety of people first, then minimise damage, then survival of the business. The Business Impact Analysis (BIA) is the foundation — it identifies critical processes and systems, sets the time objectives (RPO/RTO/WRT/MTD), quantifies impact, and gets management approval of the numbers and recovery costs. The BCP is the broad plan to keep the business operating (people, processes, facilities, comms, tech); the DRP is the narrower IT-recovery plan inside it. Restoration order is driven by criticality (most critical first) and dependency charts (you can't bring up an app before its database and network).

Exam tip: BCP ⊇ DRP. The BIA — not the plan — is the step that identifies critical functions and sets the MTD. Senior management must approve the BIA.

Sub-domain 7.14

Implement & manage physical security.

Defence in depth in the physical world — perimeter inward — plus life-safety and environmental controls.

7.14

Perimeter & Internal Controls (CPTED, Layers)

CPTEDDesign deters crime (natural surveillance)
PerimeterFence, lighting, bollards, gates, guards
EntryMantrap/turnstile defeats tailgating
People firstLife safety overrides asset protection

Physical security is layered defence in depth from the perimeter inward: CPTED (Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design) uses layout, lighting and natural surveillance to deter intruders; then fencing, bollards, lighting, gates and security guards (the most flexible control). At entry points, mantraps and turnstiles defeat tailgating/piggybacking, and badges/locks control access. Internal controls include cameras (CCTV — detective), motion sensors, and cable locks. The exam's recurring theme: human life always comes first — egress doors must fail-safe (unlock) for evacuation even if that weakens security.

7.14

Environmental & Fire Controls

PowerUPS (short) + generator (long)
HVACTemp/humidity; positive pressure
Fire triangleHeat + Fuel + Oxygen
SuppressionWater vs gas (clean agent) vs CO²

Environmental controls keep facilities running: a UPS bridges short outages and a generator covers long ones; HVAC controls temperature and humidity (too dry → static; too humid → corrosion) and uses positive pressure to keep contaminants out. For fire, remember the fire triangle (heat, fuel, oxygen — remove one to suppress). Suppression types: wet-pipe sprinklers (water, cheap but harmful to equipment), dry-pipe/pre-action (water held back to reduce accidental discharge), and clean-agent gas (e.g. FM-200/inert gases) for data centres — suppresses fire without water damage and is safe-ish for people. Detectors: smoke (ionisation/photoelectric) and heat.

People are the priority and the perimeter — protect them and train them.

7.15

Travel, Awareness, Emergencies & Duress

TravelDevice/data risk abroad; clean laptops
AwarenessInsider threat, social media, 2FA fatigue
Emergency mgmtEvacuation, occupant safety drills

Personnel safety is the human side of operations. Travel security addresses device and data risk abroad (border searches, hostile networks) — issue clean loaner laptops and use VPNs. Security training & awareness targets current threats the exam names: insider threat, social-media oversharing, and 2FA/MFA fatigue (attackers spamming push prompts until a tired user approves one — counter with number-matching). Emergency management covers evacuation and occupant safety. Duress systems give a person under coercion a covert way to signal for help — a panic button or a duress code that silently alerts security while appearing to comply. Above all, the recurring principle: human safety outranks every asset.

High-Yield Exam Cheat Sheet — Rapid-Fire Distinctions

Criminal vs civil proof: beyond a reasonable doubt vs preponderance of the evidence.

Chain of custody: any gap = inadmissible. Prove integrity with hashing.

Order of volatility: capture RAM (volatile) before disk — never pull the plug first.

IDS vs IPS: detect/alert (out-of-band) vs detect/block (inline).

Signature vs anomaly: known patterns vs baseline deviation (catches zero-day, more false positives).

Event vs incident: observable occurrence vs adverse event harming CIA.

Need-to-know vs least privilege: data vs permissions.

SoD prevents fraud; job rotation & mandatory vacation detect it.

Incremental vs differential: clears archive bit / slow restore vs keeps bit / fast restore.

RAID 5 vs 6: survives 1 vs 2 disk failures. RAID is HA, not a backup.

Cold/Warm/Hot site: days–weeks / hours–days / minutes–hours.

MTD = RTO + WRT; RPO drives backup frequency.

BCP ⊇ DRP; the BIA sets the MTD; safety of people is always goal #1.

DR tests: read-through → walkthrough → simulation → parallel → full-interruption (riskiest).

CCB/CAB/ECAB: control / advisory / emergency change approval.

Fire: never water on Class C (electrical) — use clean-agent gas in data centres.

Domain 7 in One Breath

Investigate lawfully — preserve the chain of custody, capture evidence by order of volatility, and match the proof standard to the case. Monitor everything — IDS/IPS feeding a SIEM, threat intel and hunting, UEBA — and keep assets in a known state with configuration management. Enforce least privilege, need-to-know and separation of duties, and lock down privileged accounts. Run incident management in order (prepare → detect → respond → mitigate → report → recover → remediate → learn), backed by the right detection & prevention tech (firewalls, allow-listing, sandboxing, honeypots). Patch through change management with a rollback plan. Stay resilient with backups, RAID, HA and recovery sites, and recover within the MTD (= RTO + WRT); the BIA tells you what's critical, the BCP/DRP brings it back, and you test the plan from checklist to full-interruption. Finally, secure the facility and the people — because human safety always comes first.
